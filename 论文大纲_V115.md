# Token-Local Resonance Anchoring: Bounding Entity Hallucination via Geometrically Grounded Visual Routing

## Abstract
Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) frequently suffer from entity hallucination when strong language priors overpower visual evidence during autoregressive decoding. While zero-shot inference-time mitigations (e.g., DoLa, VCD) elegantly modulate general model confidence, they lack explicit, supervised spatial-to-lexical mappings. We present **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**, a supervised late-fusion parametric head that projects post-connector visual features directly into a masked BPE vocabulary space to penalize unsupported entity tokens. 

Recognizing the algorithmic limitations of unconstrained logit manipulation, we explicitly frame TLRA as a **visual entity existence verifier** for an offline-curated physical noun dictionary ($V_{noun} \approx 4,500$). To resolve parameter bloat and semantic distortion, TLRA operates on three structural principles: (1) **Geometric Manifold Preservation**: Instead of a fully fine-tuned projection, TLRA employs a frozen calibration matrix strictly tied to the LLM's Unembedding Matrix ($W_{out}$), training only a lightweight Visual Alignment Adapter; (2) **Dynamic Activation Pooling** to resolve the spatial dilution inherent in static patch selection; and (3) **Visual-Aware Syntax Maintenance (VASM)** via an Intervention Abort Threshold ($\tau$), mathematically guaranteeing that hallucination suppression does not force irreversible grammatical collapse. To resolve BPE fragmentation and the subword collateral damage fallacy, we introduce **Deterministic Vocabulary Trie Masking**, isolating penalties to specific noun sequences without degrading homophonic prefixes (e.g., preserving `_really` while penalizing `_refrigerator`). Finally, we explicitly confront the **Negation Trap**—the inherent failure of context-blind visual checkers to permit accurate textual refusals—by implementing a contextual kill-switch and pre-registering a rigorous audit separating explicit and implicit negations. We benchmark against prompt-injected object detection (e.g., Grounding DINO) and profile system-level latency to justify the necessity and viability of late-fusion intervention.

## 1. Introduction

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit remarkable generative capabilities, yet their autoregressive decoding behavior remains vulnerable to entity hallucination. Current inference-time mitigation strategies (e.g., DoLa, VCD) are powerful training-free methods that modulate general model confidence via contrastive decoding or distribution shifts. However, as purely zero-shot interventions, they lack explicit, supervised spatial-to-lexical mapping pathways trained specifically to verify spatial presence at the token level. 

We introduce **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**. Crucially, TLRA is *not* a zero-shot decoding trick; it is a late-fusion parametric routing module requiring targeted supervision. By training a dedicated mapping from spatial features to lexical logits on a masked-caption subset—with the base LLM completely frozen—TLRA introduces explicit existence-checking supervision.

To maintain intellectual rigor, we explicitly downgrade our scope from general "hallucination suppression" to **Entity Existence Verification**. Algorithmically, TLRA acts as an existence checker for an offline-curated dictionary of physical noun stems ($V_{noun} \approx 4,500$). This precise formulation forces us to confront four hard theoretical boundaries:
First, against **Prompt-Injected Object Detection**: if TLRA cannot outperform an off-the-shelf detector (e.g., Grounding DINO) appending a text list of objects to the prompt without destroying generation throughput, its architectural complexity is unjustified. 
Second, TLRA risks **exacerbating relational hallucinations**. Verifying that a "sofa" exists in the image may inadvertently validate an incorrect spatial relation (e.g., "dog on the sofa") generated by the base LLM's self-attention vulnerabilities.
Third, the **Negation Trap**: A strictly context-blind visual checker will disastrously penalize correct negative constraints. If the LLM correctly attempts to generate "There is no dog," a context-blind TLRA will suppress "dog" and force a syntactic pivot (e.g., "There is no cat"). 
Fourth, the **BPE Collateral Damage Fallacy**: Naive subword suppression risks morphological destruction. Penalizing the subword `_re` because "refrigerator" is missing visually will arbitrarily destroy non-noun vocabulary like `_really` or `_return`.

To resolve these threats, TLRA introduces **Deterministic Vocabulary Trie Masking** to safely route BPE probabilities, and **Visual-Aware Syntax Maintenance (VASM)** equipped with an Intervention Abort Threshold ($\tau$) to gracefully concede a hallucination rather than forcing grammatical collapse. We prevent **Geometric Manifold Drift** by anchoring the projection to the LLM's frozen Unembedding Matrix ($W_{out}$), and replace static Top-$k$ pooling with **Dynamic Activation Thresholding**.

Our evaluation is a falsifiable protocol organized into **three evidence chains and five defenses**:
1. **Hallucination Reduction vs. The Negation Trap (POPE / CHAIR):** Evaluating entity grounding against zero-shot methods and Prompt-Injected OD, while explicitly auditing True Negative Accuracy across *explicit* and *implicit* negations to measure context-blindness vulnerabilities.
2. **Structure, Reasoning, and BPE Preservation (MMBench / MME / MMMU / DocVQA):** Auditing BPE collateral damage and the structural protection of VASM (`TLRA_no_VASM` vs Base) on text-heavy and reasoning tasks.
3. **Local Evidence Value and System Efficiency (FREAK / System Latency):** Comparing `TLRA_zero` against Mean-Pooled bounds to prove dynamic local extraction, and rigorously benchmarking tokens/second across expanding $V_{noun}$ sizes.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Decode-Time Hallucination Mitigation

Inference-time mitigation for MLLM hallucination has developed along several lines. Contrastive methods such as `VCD` compare logits under different visual conditions. Attention-based methods such as `OPERA` detect abnormal generation patterns during decoding. Layer-contrast methods such as `DoLa` compare distributions from different network depths. These methods demonstrate that hallucination can be mitigated at inference time, but they rely on different intervention signals and carry different robustness and latency trade-offs.

### 2.2 Local Evidence vs. Coarse Internal Control

High-resolution MLLMs operate over large spatial token fields rather than a single compact visual descriptor. For dense grounding tasks, the useful evidence for a word is often concentrated in a small region. This makes token-local evidence attractive: if the next token is visually supported, the support is often sparse, local, and candidate-dependent. TLRA is built around the claim that decode-time hallucination mitigation should explicitly test that locality.

This is not a sweeping claim about all external baselines. It is a narrower methodological claim: when a task depends on local visual evidence, a framework that explicitly reweights candidate logits using localized support should be tested against coarser internal alternatives under matched conditions.

### 2.3 Structure Preservation in Decoding

A recurring difficulty in token-level intervention is that not all tokens should be treated equally. Function words, discourse markers, and continuation fragments play a structural role distinct from visually grounded entity tokens. A practical decode-time method must therefore distinguish hallucination suppression from indiscriminate token suppression. TLRA addresses this via a deterministic structural mask rather than entropy-only heuristics.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Connector Locus and the Manifold Preservation Principle

TLRA operates as a supervised parametric intervention injected at the late-fusion (decode) stage, strictly limited to an offline-curated subset of physical noun stems ($V_{noun} \approx 4,500$). 

**The Connector Locus Requirement:** To map visual features to the LLM's vocabulary space, the spatial features $X_v$ *must* be extracted **post-vision-language connector** (e.g., after the LLaVA MLP projector). Raw vision encoder features (e.g., CLIP) exist in an incompatible dimensional manifold.

**The Sparsity Trap and Manifold Preservation:** To enable zero-shot generalization to unseen tokens, the projection must inherit semantic structure. We utilize the base LLM's pre-trained **Unembedding Matrix ($W_{out}$)** sliced for $V_{noun}$ as our anchor matrix $W_{anchor} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times V_{noun}}$. 
Crucially, if $W_{anchor}$ is updated during training, its weights will immediately drift away from the LLM's exact logit space. Therefore, **$W_{anchor}$ is strictly frozen**. We train a lightweight, low-rank Visual Alignment Adapter $f_{adapt}(X_v)$ on a 50k Visual Genome subset. 

**`TLRA_zero` & `TLRA_MeanPool`:** To isolate spatial localization capacity, `TLRA_zero` computes cosine similarity directly between frozen post-connector features and $W_{anchor}$ without $f_{adapt}$. To prove that dynamic localization matters over global context, we define `TLRA_MeanPool`, which violently averages all $X_v$ tokens before projection.

### 3.2 Contrastive Calibration and Dynamic Activation Pooling

To calibrate $f_{adapt}$, we employ a **Contrastive Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) Loss** dynamically sampling hard negatives constrained by a **Semantic Margin $\phi_{sim}$** (via frozen CLIP text-text similarity) to prevent penalizing visual synonyms.

Visual patches are not bag-of-words. Static Top-$k$ pooling dilutes the existence signal of small objects. We utilize **Dynamic Activation Thresholding**. At decoding step $t$, for a candidate noun $c$, we identify the subset of active spatial patches $\mathcal{A}_c = \{j \mid (f_{adapt}(X_{v, j}) \cdot W_{anchor})_c > \theta_{pool}\}$. The spatial support is the mean of these dynamically selected patches:
$$
S_{raw}(c) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}_c|} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{A}_c} (f_{adapt}(X_{v, j}) \cdot W_{anchor})_c \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat S(c) = \text{Clamp}\Big(\sigma\big(S_{raw}(c) \cdot \beta\big), 0, 1\Big)
$$
If $\mathcal{A}_c$ is empty, $S_{raw}(c) = 0$.

### 3.3 Deterministic Vocabulary Trie Masking (Solving BPE Collateral Damage)

Modern tokenizers shatter words ambiguously. The naive approach of penalizing a subword prefix (e.g., `_re` for `_refrigerator`) causes unacceptable BPE collateral damage by suppressing homophonic non-nouns (e.g., `_really`, `_return`). 

We replace probability-based subword lookahead with **Deterministic Vocabulary Trie Masking**. We construct a deterministic character-to-token Trie of $V_{noun}$. At step $t$, TLRA only applies a penalty to a subword logit if that subword deterministically routes *exclusively* to nodes within $V_{noun}$ that lack visual support $\hat S(c)$. If a subword branch contains *any* valid non-noun continuation (e.g., the path for `_re`), the visual penalty is deferred. The penalty $\gamma(c)$ only engages when the autoregressive trajectory enters a Trie branch topologically isolated to a hallucinated physical entity, mathematically guaranteeing zero collateral damage to general vocabulary.

### 3.4 Visual-Aware Syntax Maintenance (VASM) and The Negation Trap

**The $\tau$-Abort Syntax Floor:** Let $L_{floor}$ be the highest logit of any non-noun token currently in the top-$K$ window. Forcing the argmax to fall to $L_{floor}$ is fatal if $L_{floor}$ represents a grammatically invalid token (e.g., a comma). The **Visual-Aware Syntax Maintenance (VASM)** module enforces an Intervention Abort Threshold ($\tau$):
$$
L_{final}(c) = 
\begin{cases} 
L_{orig}(c) & \text{if } (L_{orig}(c) - L_{floor}) > \tau \\
\max \Big( L_{floor} - \epsilon, \, L_{orig}(c) - \alpha \cdot \gamma(c) \cdot (1 - \hat S(c)) \Big) & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$
If the entropy gap exceeds $\tau$, VASM aborts the penalty. Permitting a hallucinated noun is strictly preferable to irreversible structural collapse.

**The Explicit Negation Kill-Switch:** A context-blind checker mathematically destroys the LLM's ability to output accurate negative constraints. We introduce a lightweight look-behind heuristic: if an explicit negation token ("no", "not", "without") appears in $Y_{t-\delta:t}$, the TLRA penalty $\gamma(c)$ is forced to 0. We formally acknowledge this is a brittle heuristic that will likely fail on implicit negations (e.g., "absent", "omitted"), bounding the method's real-world reliability.

## 5. Discussion and Limitations

The viability of Token-Local Resonance Anchoring depends entirely on surviving strict linguistic, structural, and hardware boundaries. By reframing TLRA as a geometrically grounded entity existence verifier, we identify mathematically rigid limitations that define the method's frontier:

1. **The Implicit Negation Boundary:** The introduction of the Explicit Negation Kill-Switch mathematically bounds the "Negation Trap," but remains a heuristic patch. As hypothesized in Chain A, a context-blind existence checker will systematically fail on complex implicit negations (e.g., "The image lacks a dog"). Late-fusion visual routing fundamentally conflicts with deep autoregressive textual reasoning during subtle refusals.
2. **The BPE Determinism Trade-off:** While Deterministic Vocabulary Trie Masking successfully prevents collateral damage to non-noun subwords, it creates a latency overhead proportional to the Trie depth and $V_{noun}$ size. If system latency (evaluated in Chain C) drops significantly below Prompt-Injected baselines, the utility of decode-time intervention diminishes.
3. **Relational Exacerbation vs. Global Existence:** Because TLRA pools visual patches independently of deep text history, it excels at global entity verification but actively risks *exacerbating* relational hallucinations. Validating an object's existence inherently risks strengthening an incorrect positional claim made by the base LLM.
4. **The Grammatical Trade-off (VASM):** The Visual-Aware Syntax Maintenance threshold formally concedes that late-fusion logit manipulation cannot always gracefully recover from hallucination triggers. If the sequence entropy is too low, the system must allow a hallucination to pass to prevent catastrophic grammatical destruction. 

Future execution of this protocol will definitively confirm whether a geometrically-coherent decode-time routing head—protected by Vocabulary Tries and VASM—can safely navigate these morphological fragilities without sacrificing inference speed.
