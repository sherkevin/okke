# Token-Local Resonance Anchoring: A Budget-Matched Decode-Time Routing Head for Visual Existence Verification in MLLMs

## Abstract

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) frequently hallucinate when strong language priors overpower visual evidence during entity grounding. While zero-shot inference-time mitigations modulate internal model confidence, they lack explicitly trained cross-modal pathways. We present **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**, a late-fusion parametric head that projects visual patches directly into BPE vocabulary logits to penalize unsupported entity tokens during autoregressive decoding. 

Recognizing the mathematical limitations of unconstrained logit manipulation, we explicitly frame TLRA as a **context-independent visual existence verifier** rather than a complex spatial reasoner. It reliably answers "Is this noun in the image?" to suppress pure hallucinations, trading relational reasoning for robust, decode-time entity anchoring. To justify its $\sim$131M parameter footprint, TLRA is subjected to a strict "budget-matched" evaluation against a high-rank prefill-time LoRA adaptation, enforcing identical 50k masked-caption supervision. TLRA operates on three principles: (1) A calibrated projection head navigating a strict static vs. dynamic memory footprint tradeoff (TTFT vs. TPOT); (2) Absolute Evidence Scoring over bounded candidate windows; and (3) Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM). VASM restricts interventions to physical nouns via an offline BPE-level dictionary, employing a **Subword Continuation Rule** explicitly audited via a novel Morphological Error Rate (MER) metric to prevent "Frankenstein words." This work establishes a pre-registered, boundary-aware protocol for parameter-efficient decode-time visual routing, explicitly mapping its structural, morphological, and relational failure boundaries.

## 1. Introduction

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit remarkable generative capabilities, yet their autoregressive decoding behavior remains vulnerable to hallucination. In fine-grained physical grounding scenarios, global image representations often fail to provide the highly localized evidence required to anchor specific generated tokens.

Current inference-time mitigation strategies (e.g., DoLa, VCD) are powerful zero-shot methods that modulate general model confidence via contrastive decoding or layer-wise distribution shifts. However, because they are zero-shot, they cannot introduce new cross-modal alignment pathways. Conversely, standard parametric approaches (like LoRA) operate entirely in the prefill phase, compressing spatial evidence into the global context window without explicitly routing it to specific token generation steps. This leaves a critical gap: **How can we inject fine-grained, token-specific visual evidence directly into decode-time logits adjustment without destroying morphological syntax or hitting severe memory bandwidth walls?**

We introduce **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**. Crucially, TLRA is *not* a zero-shot decoding trick; it is explicitly formulated as a late-fusion parametric fine-tuning head. TLRA extracts local spatial support to bias decode-time logits, strictly limited to entity-centric grounding. By training a dedicated mapping from spatial features to lexical logits on a 50k masked-caption subset, TLRA introduces explicit supervision that zero-shot methods lack. 

To maintain intellectual rigor, we explicitly reject the framing of TLRA as a "context-aware spatial reasoner." Mathematically, TLRA acts as a **Global Visual Existence Checker**. By projecting patch features into the vocabulary space independently of the LLM's text history ($Y_{<t}$), TLRA robustly verifies whether a candidate noun exists *anywhere* in the image. While this effectively suppresses pure hallucinations, it establishes a hard theoretical boundary: TLRA cannot resolve multi-instance or relational spatial queries (e.g., distinguishing a "red car on the left" from a "blue car on the right"). We embrace this constraint, demonstrating that context-blind existence checking is highly effective for core hallucination reduction if properly bounded.

Furthermore, we dispel the "lightweight" illusion of vocabulary-space projections. For a standard architecture ($D=4096$, $V=32000$), TLRA introduces $\sim$131M parameters. To justify this architectural complexity, TLRA must survive a "do-or-die" evaluation: it must strictly outperform a data-matched, mathematically budget-matched (high-rank) prefill LoRA adaptation.

To prevent structural collapse, TLRA introduces Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM), an offline BPE-level dictionary explicitly mapping physical entity synsets to binary subword masks. To mitigate the risk of morphological text corruption ("Frankenstein words") and out-of-vocabulary brittleness, VASM enforces a Subword Continuation Rule, restricting interventions to the initial stems of groundable nouns.

To ensure methodological rigor, we structure the evaluation as a falsifiable audit organized into **three evidence chains and five defenses**:
1. **Hallucination Reduction:** Evaluating standard entity grounding (POPE, CHAIR) using strictly disjoint training data, while auditing morphological syntax preservation via Average Generation Length (AGL) and Morphological Error Rate (MER).
2. **Structure and Reasoning Preservation:** Auditing VASM's protection of general capabilities (MMBench) and evaluating the performance of the budget-matched `Base + LoRA` control.
3. **Local Evidence Value & Relational Boundaries:** Isolating spatial advantage on FREAK against a matched-parameter `Global_Visual_State` control, while explicitly documenting TLRA's failure boundary on a Multi-Instance/Relational stress test.
4. **Semantic Boundaries and OOD Fallback:** Bounding the method via an Action/Verb stress test and verifying graceful degradation on Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOD) jargon.
5. **Hardware Bandwidth Physics:** A formal evaluation of the static vs. dynamic memory footprint, mapping the tradeoff between Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) and Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT).

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Decode-Time Hallucination Mitigation

Inference-time mitigation for MLLM hallucination has developed along several lines. Contrastive methods such as `VCD` compare logits under different visual conditions. Attention-based methods such as `OPERA` detect abnormal generation patterns during decoding. Layer-contrast methods such as `DoLa` compare distributions from different network depths. These methods demonstrate that hallucination can be mitigated at inference time, but they rely on different intervention signals and carry different robustness and latency trade-offs.

### 2.2 Local Evidence vs. Coarse Internal Control

High-resolution MLLMs operate over large spatial token fields rather than a single compact visual descriptor. For dense grounding tasks, the useful evidence for a word is often concentrated in a small region. This makes token-local evidence attractive: if the next token is visually supported, the support is often sparse, local, and candidate-dependent. TLRA is built around the claim that decode-time hallucination mitigation should explicitly test that locality.

This is not a sweeping claim about all external baselines. It is a narrower methodological claim: when a task depends on local visual evidence, a framework that explicitly reweights candidate logits using localized support should be tested against coarser internal alternatives under matched conditions.

### 2.3 Structure Preservation in Decoding

A recurring difficulty in token-level intervention is that not all tokens should be treated equally. Function words, discourse markers, and continuation fragments play a structural role distinct from visually grounded entity tokens. A practical decode-time method must therefore distinguish hallucination suppression from indiscriminate token suppression. TLRA addresses this via a deterministic structural mask rather than entropy-only heuristics.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Architectural Scope, Parameter Accounting, and the Late-Fusion Head

TLRA targets standard ViT + MLP projector architectures where spatial provenance is preserved across visual tokens $X_v$. TLRA operates as a parametric intervention injected at the late-fusion (decode) stage:

**1. `TLRA_calib` (The Parametric Routing Head):** A linear projection matrix $W_{calib} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times V}$ maps cached spatial visual states of dimension $D$ directly to the lexical vocabulary size $V$. For a standard model (e.g., Llama-2 with $D=4096$, $V=32000$), $W_{calib}$ constitutes $\sim$131 million parameters. 
- **Training Formulation:** $W_{calib}$ is trained on a bounded subset of 50k conceptual captions. The base LLM and Vision Encoder remain entirely frozen. The objective is standard next-token prediction cross-entropy, but the loss is **strictly masked** to only backpropagate on tokens where VASM $\gamma(y_t) = 1$ (physical entities). $\mathcal{L}_{calib} = -\sum_{t} \gamma(y_t) \log P(y_t \mid X_v, Y_{<t})$. 
- **The Parametric Baseline Contract:** Because 131M parameters significantly exceeds standard LoRA configurations (typically 4-8M parameters), TLRA *must* be compared against a high-rank (e.g., $r \ge 256$) budget-matched LoRA adapter trained on the exact same masked objective.

**2. `TLRA_zero` (Representation Sanity Check):** Computes cosine similarity directly between frozen post-projector visual features ($X_v$) and the LLM's discrete text embeddings ($E_{in}$). Because the ViT/MLP features are optimized to align with the LLM's *input* layers rather than its output vocabulary space, this mapping is geometrically incoherent. `TLRA_zero` mathematically proves that decode-time spatial routing fundamentally requires the learned $W_{calib}$ projection to overcome this representation anisotropy.

### 3.2 The Hardware Bandwidth Tradeoff: Static vs. Dynamic Memory Footprint

At each decoding step $t$, TLRA maps spatial features $X_v$ to lexical biases for a bounded candidate set $\mathcal{C}_t = \text{Top-}M(L_{orig})$. Computing this routing introduces memory bandwidth challenges. We define two implementations to balance Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) and Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT):

**A. Static Precomputation (Optimizing TPOT over TTFT):** During prefill, cached visual states are mapped to the full vocabulary, yielding a static bias matrix $B = X_v \cdot W_{calib}$, where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{N_v \times V}$. This incurs a one-time FLOP penalty that degrades TTFT, but gathering arbitrary token columns $c \in \mathcal{C}_t$ during generation limits step computation to uncoalesced memory reads from High Bandwidth Memory (HBM).
**B. Dynamic Sliced-Matmul (Optimizing static footprint over FLOPs):** We dynamically compute the projection *only* for the Top-$M$ candidates at each step: $B_{step} = X_v \cdot W_{calib}[:, \mathcal{C}_t]$. For $N_v \approx 3000$, the static footprint of $B$ is $\sim$192MB (fp16). The true bottleneck is not this static VRAM capacity, but the HBM bandwidth saturated by uncoalesced gather operations. Dynamic sliced-matmul trades FLOPs to avoid these uncoalesced reads.

### 3.3 The Context-Blind "Existence Checker" and Logit Clipping

We explicitly define the mathematical boundary of TLRA: it acts as a **Global Existence Checker**, not a context-aware spatial reasoner. TLRA computes a raw spatial support score by pooling the top-$k$ matching patches for a candidate token $c$:
$$
S_{raw}(c) = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{j \in \text{Top-}k} B_{j, c} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat S(c) = \text{Clamp}\Big(\sigma\big(S_{raw}(c) \cdot \beta\big), 0, 1\Big)
$$
Crucially, $S_{raw}$ has no dependence on the text history $Y_{<t}$ outside of the LLM's candidate proposal window. It verifies if visual evidence for candidate $c$ exists *anywhere* in the image. While this effectively penalizes pure hallucinations, it theoretically guarantees failure on multi-instance/relational queries (e.g., distinguishing identical objects with different attributes), as both valid and invalid targets will return high $S_{raw}$ scores.

To apply this evidence without forcing structural collapse, we compute a clipped penalty. We define $\Delta_L = L_{orig}(c_1) - L_{orig}(c_2)$ as the margin between the base model's top-1 and top-2 logits. The final adjusted logit is:
$$
L_{final}(c) = L_{orig}(c) - \alpha \cdot \gamma(c) \cdot \min(\Delta_L, \delta_{max}) \cdot (1 - \hat S(c))
$$
Bounding the penalty by $\Delta_L$ ensures that when the base model is overwhelmingly confident in syntax (e.g., a massive $\Delta_L$), TLRA cannot arbitrarily flip the argmax, protecting probability mass shifts from inadvertently boosting verbs or generic tokens.

### 3.4 VASM, Tokenizer Leakiness, and the Subword Continuation Rule

To preserve syntax, TLRA utilizes VASM, an offline deterministic binary mask ($\gamma \in \{0, 1\}^V$) extracted from English WordNet physical entity synsets.

**The "Frankenstein" Word Risk:** Multi-token entities (e.g., "refrigerator" $\rightarrow$ `_refrig`, `erator`) pose a severe morphological risk. If TLRA dramatically alters the logit of the prefix `_refrig`, the base model's internal state may fail to naturally output `erator`. TLRA enforces a **Subword Continuation Rule**: $\gamma$ is set to $1$ *only* for the initial subword stem. By anchoring semantic routing purely on the prefix, the base LLM completes the morphological suffix uninterrupted, audited via a Morphological Error Rate (MER).

**Data Isolation Guarantee & Baseline Protocol:** To ensure a mathematically valid comparison against zero-shot methods, the 50k training split for $W_{calib}$ is drawn from a filtered subset of Visual Genome, strictly verified to have zero image-level overlap with the COCO validation sets used in POPE and CHAIR. Furthermore, the `Base + LoRA` control is trained on the exact same *VASM-masked* loss as $W_{calib}$, ensuring identical optimization targets.

## 5. Discussion and Limitations

The viability of Token-Local Resonance Anchoring depends entirely on surviving strict computational, structural, and relational boundaries. By explicitly reframing TLRA as a targeted, context-independent visual existence verifier, we identify the following mathematically rigid limitations:

1. **The "Existence Checker" Fallacy (Relational Blindness):** The mathematical formulation of $S_{raw}$ (Eq 1) pools patches independently of text context. While highly effective at verifying if a noun exists in an image, TLRA cannot resolve multi-instance or relational spatial queries. It trades complex reasoning for robust, lightweight decode-time hallucination suppression.
2. **The Parametric Baseline Mandate:** At $\sim$131M parameters, TLRA is not "lightweight" compared to standard adapters. If it fails to outperform a strictly budget-matched, data-matched high-rank LoRA adapter operating in the prefill phase, the architectural complexity of decode-time routing is rendered unjustifiable.
3. **The Brittleness of VASM:** Relying on an offline WordNet dictionary mapped to a specific BPE tokenizer inherently limits scalability. TLRA is structurally vulnerable to multilingual tasks, domain-specific jargon (medical/industrial), out-of-vocabulary slang, or migrations to newer tokenizers (e.g., transitioning from Llama-2's 32k to Llama-3's 128k vocabulary).
4. **The Memory Bandwidth Tradeoff (TTFT vs. TPOT):** Implementing decode-time spatial routing introduces unavoidable hardware friction. Static precomputation front-loads FLOPs (degrading TTFT) and causes uncoalesced HBM reads during generation (degrading TPOT). Dynamic sliced-matmul avoids the static footprint but incurs dynamic FLOP overhead.
5. **Top-M Unrecoverability (The Hijacking CDF):** The intervention is mathematically constrained by the base model's candidate window $\mathcal{C}_t$. If strong language priors marginalize the ground-truth visual token below the Top-$M$ cutoff prior to intervention, our absolute evidence penalty cannot rescue it.

Future execution of this pre-registered protocol will definitively confirm whether a trained decode-time routing head can overcome the inherent morphological fragility, relational blindness, and HBM bandwidth walls to provide scalable, targeted visual grounding.
