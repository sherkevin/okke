# CHORD: Calibrating Hallucinations via Object-Resonant Decoding in Multimodal Large Language Models

## Abstract
Multimodal Large Language Models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in multi-modal understanding and generation. However, they persistently suffer from hallucination issues, generating text that contradicts visual inputs. The fundamental root cause of these hallucinations lies in the cross-modal granularity mismatch during autoregressive decoding: while MLLMs encode images as continuous holistic features, they are forced to generate text as discrete, often semantically fragmented sub-word tokens. This structural misalignment makes it mathematically intractable to ground individual, meaningless tokens to concrete visual entities. Consequently, the model is forced into an unverified decoding state, where the statistical inertia of language priors inevitably breeds hallucinations. Existing mitigation strategies struggle to address this efficiently, as they typically require computationally expensive model retraining or incur severe inference overhead through complex multi-round verification.Motivated by this insight, we propose CHORD, a training-free, plug-and-play decoding framework designed to forcefully re-anchor text generation to visual reality via Object-Resonant Decoding. Inspired by the physical resonance process, CHORD intercepts the active decoding frontier and dynamically evaluates the alignment between candidate text spans and external visual evidence. A candidate span's generation probability is amplified only if it semantically "resonates" with a concrete visual object, whereas ungrounded hallucinations—driven merely by linguistic inertia—are decisively penalized. Furthermore, to prevent over-correction and maintain native inference efficiency, we introduce an adaptive entropy-aware gating strategy that selectively triggers interventions only during highly uncertain generation steps. Extensive experiments across multiple hallucination-focused benchmarks demonstrate that CHORD substantially reduces object hallucinations. As a model-agnostic framework, it requires no additional training and establishes a highly scalable paradigm for trustworthy MLLM deployment.

## 1. Introduction

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have rapidly advanced visual question answering, captioning, and open-ended multimodal reasoning. Yet even strong models remain vulnerable to hallucination: they may generate fluent but weakly grounded statements, invent objects absent from the image, or confidently choose visually unsupported entities under strong language priors. These failures are particularly damaging when the correct evidence is sparse, local, or easily drowned out by semantically irrelevant visual context.

Recent hallucination-mitigation methods have shown that inference-time intervention is a promising alternative to retraining the host model. Contrastive decoding methods such as `VCD`, attention-based control methods such as `OPERA`, and layer-comparison methods such as `DoLa` all demonstrate that decoding is a meaningful intervention point. However, these approaches leave unresolved a different and more demanding design question:

**Can one construct a learned grounding-control mechanism that attaches at decode time and remains reusable across heterogeneous MLLM families without model-specific retraining?**

This question is fundamentally different from improving a single host model. A module may be lightweight yet still be non-portable if its semantics are inherited from the host architecture. In particular, any learned component that consumes model-specific multimodal hidden states and emits corrections directly in model-specific lexical geometry is coupled to the host model, even when the backbone itself is frozen. Under this criterion, the earlier `Phi_calib` route is scientifically useful, but it does not satisfy a strict universality requirement.

The central claim of this paper is therefore a **universality boundary**: strict hot-swappability is impossible when the learned module lives inside model-native hidden, logit, or attention spaces. To make universality a falsifiable scientific claim rather than a loose engineering intuition, the learned component must instead operate in a standardized external space built from frozen public vision embeddings, frozen public text embeddings, decoded candidate spans, and deterministic attachment logic. This observation leads to **UniGround**, whose learned core `Psi_univ` is external to the host MLLM and whose runtime interface is parameter-free.

UniGround is motivated by a more specific insight than generic decode-time correction. When the host model is about to emit a visually grounded object token, the right question is not whether the whole unfinished prefix is globally similar to the image, but whether the **candidate itself** can wake up the right local evidence from the image and whether that awakened evidence is strong enough to justify intervention. We call this behavior **candidate-driven visual resonance**. It reframes hallucination mitigation as selective external evidence activation: the controller should intervene only when a visually groundable candidate is risky, and only using evidence awakened by that candidate rather than by unrelated regions or purely textual continuation bias.

This perspective yields two immediate consequences. First, the learned module must be judged not only by its raw mitigation effect, but also by whether the **same checkpoint** transfers across different host models without learned adaptation. Second, a purportedly token-local universal method must outperform simpler alternatives such as a static global visual prior; otherwise the additional mechanism is not justified.

The paper makes four contributions:

1. It formalizes a **universality boundary** showing why hidden-state-to-lexical-space calibrators cannot support a strict universal plugin claim.
2. It proposes **UniGround**, a universal external sidecar that performs candidate-driven visual resonance and conservative object-level grounding control in a shared semantic space.
3. It introduces a **parameter-free tokenizer bridge** that attaches the same learned sidecar to heterogeneous MLLMs during decoding without touching host-internal attention.
4. It defines an **evaluation protocol** that separates module-level validation from system-level hot-swap results, while auditing global-prior baselines, structure preservation, portability-cost tradeoffs, and explicit failure boundaries.

The remainder of the paper follows this logic. Section 2 situates UniGround within the contemporary literature on multimodal hallucination evaluation and mitigation. Section 3 positions UniGround relative to the earlier TLRA line and formalizes the universality boundary. Section 4 presents the universal controller, with particular emphasis on grounding resonance and safety-gated frontier redistribution. Section 5 specifies the evaluation protocol required to distinguish universal candidate-aware control from both model-coupled calibration and simpler global priors. Sections 6 and 7 then organize the main empirical evidence, its interpretation, and the scope of the resulting claims.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Multimodal hallucination evaluation and benchmark design

The modern study of multimodal hallucination rests on two complementary evaluation traditions. The first is the object-hallucination line represented by `CHAIR` in *Object Hallucination in Image Captioning* (EMNLP 2018), which established the importance of measuring whether generated objects are actually licensed by the image rather than merely rewarded by language overlap. The second is the probing-based LVLM line represented by `POPE` in *Evaluating Object Hallucination in Large Vision-Language Models* (EMNLP 2023), which reframed hallucination measurement around controlled yes/no object-existence probes and exposed the sensitivity of open-ended generation to object co-occurrence bias. More recent benchmarks such as `AMBER` extend this perspective toward multi-dimensional evaluation spanning existence, attribute, and relation hallucinations.

These benchmarks are not interchangeable, and the paper should not treat them as such. `CHAIR` remains useful when the claim concerns object-level faithfulness in descriptive generation, whereas `POPE` is better aligned with controlled hallucination probing under a shared prompt template. `AMBER` broadens the scope further by asking whether object hallucination is only the visible surface of a larger multimodal faithfulness problem. UniGround is designed with this benchmark ecology in mind: its central claim is not tied to a single metric, but its strongest evidence should come from settings where hallucination can be measured under explicit grounding-sensitive protocols rather than solely through generic answer-quality scores.

### 2.2 Decode-time mitigation and control

The closest neighbors to UniGround are inference-time methods that intervene during generation rather than retraining the host model. `VCD` (*Mitigating Object Hallucinations in Large Vision-Language Models through Visual Contrastive Decoding*, CVPR 2024) contrasts distributions induced by clean and distorted visual inputs in order to suppress language-prior-driven objects. `OPERA` (*Alleviating Hallucination in Multi-Modal Large Language Models via Over-Trust Penalty and Retrospection-Allocation*, CVPR 2024) monitors generation dynamics through attention and retrospection signals, then penalizes trajectories associated with hallucination. `DoLa` (*Decoding by Contrasting Layers Improves Factuality in Large Language Models*, ICLR 2024) originates on the LLM side and shows that layerwise logit contrast can improve factual generation without parameter updates; when transferred into multimodal settings, it serves as a strong decode-time baseline rather than a grounding controller in the present sense.

What unifies these methods is not a shared mechanism, but a shared operational insight: decoding is a meaningful intervention surface. What separates them from UniGround is the scientific object being optimized. Existing decode-time methods typically alter generation through contrastive inputs, attention-level penalties, or host-internal distribution comparisons. UniGround instead asks whether a **single learned controller** can be reused across hosts while remaining external to model-native hidden geometry. The relevant comparison is therefore not simply whether UniGround changes logits, but whether it does so under a stricter portability contract than methods whose intervention signals are inherited from a specific host model or inference configuration.

### 2.3 Post-hoc correction, model-updating mitigation, and UniGround's position

Another important family addresses hallucination by changing the response after generation or by updating the model itself. `LURE` (*Analyzing and Mitigating Object Hallucination in Large Vision-Language Models*, ICLR 2024) performs lightweight post-hoc revision grounded in hallucination statistics. `Woodpecker` (*Hallucination Correction for Multimodal Large Language Models*, arXiv 2023) decomposes the response into verifiable claims and corrects hallucinations through a multi-stage validation pipeline. `Volcano` (*Mitigating Multimodal Hallucination through Self-Feedback Guided Revision*, NAACL 2024) uses self-feedback and iterative revision, while `HA-DPO` (*Beyond Hallucinations: Enhancing LVLMs through Hallucination-Aware Direct Preference Optimization*, arXiv 2023) tackles hallucination through preference-based model updating.

These approaches are valuable precisely because they solve a different problem. Post-hoc correction methods can afford richer verification pipelines because they need not operate inside the host decoder's per-step budget; training-based methods can absorb stronger supervision because they are allowed to change model behavior permanently. UniGround does neither. Its target is the narrower but scientifically sharper regime of **portable decode-time grounding control**: the same learned sidecar should be reusable across host models, no learned per-model attachment is permitted, and any host dependence must be confined to deterministic interface logic. This places UniGround at a distinct point in the design space, between host-coupled decode-time interventions on one side and post-hoc or training-based mitigation on the other.

## 3. Positioning and Design Principle

### 3.1 Continuity with earlier TLRA

The earlier TLRA line established three principles that remain central here. First, hallucination mitigation should act **during generation**, not only through post-hoc filtering. Second, the relevant unit of control is **candidate-local**, because hallucination often emerges at the level of specific lexical alternatives rather than global sequence quality. Third, any practical decode-time intervention must preserve linguistic structure rather than indiscriminately suppress visually weak tokens.

UniGround preserves these principles while changing the location and role of the learned component. It is formulated as a decode-time grounding-control interface rather than as a theory of how evidence should be rerouted inside the host MLLM. This distinction is substantive. The goal is not to reinterpret the host model's internal reasoning trajectory, but to attach a portable external controller that can evaluate and modulate candidate spans under a strict universality constraint.

### 3.2 Why internal lexical alignment is not universal

A learned module fails to be strictly universal if either its inputs or outputs are host-specific. More precisely, universality is broken when:

1. the module consumes hidden states whose semantics depend on the host architecture; or
2. the module emits vectors interpreted directly in the host model's lexical space.

The point can be formalized directly. Let `m` denote a host MLLM with hidden-state space `H_m`, vocabulary `V_m`, and unembedding map `U_m`. Suppose a learned controller `f_\theta` consumes `h_t \in H_m` and emits a correction in the host lexical space `\mathbb{R}^{|V_m|}`. If there is no model-independent isomorphism that simultaneously aligns `H_m`, `H_{m'}`, `V_m`, and `V_{m'}` across two hosts `m` and `m'`, then the same parameters `\theta` cannot preserve semantic equivalence across both models. The learned mapping is therefore host-coupled by construction.

`Phi_calib` instantiates exactly this host-coupled pattern. It reads model-native multimodal hidden states and projects them into the active model's lexical geometry. Its behavior therefore depends on hidden-state provenance, vocabulary structure, tokenizer conventions, and `lm_head` organization. It remains an informative internal control, but it cannot ground a strict universal-plugin claim.

### 3.3 Universality through standardized observation and action spaces

To make universality defensible, the learned plugin must instead be defined through model-agnostic observation and action spaces:

- **Observation space:** pixels, frozen image embeddings, frozen text embeddings, decoded strings, and optional frozen region proposals.
- **Action space:** span-level estimates of visual support, contradiction, and abstention.

Under this design, the learned plugin never consumes model-native hidden states, never reads `lm_head.weight`, and never relies on a learned model-specific bridge. Universality is thus enforced jointly at the level of representation and attachment. The method is universal not because every component is identical across models, but because all model-dependent variation is confined to deterministic, non-learned attachment logic.

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Problem setup and active decoding frontier

Let `m` denote a host MLLM with vocabulary `V_m`. Given an image `I` and a text prefix `y_{<t}`, the host model produces next-token logits

$$
L_t^{(m)} \in \mathbb{R}^{|V_m|}.
$$

Rather than attempting to override the full vocabulary distribution with an external model, UniGround acts on the host model's **active semantic frontier**

$$
\mathcal{F}_t^{(m)} = \mathrm{Top}\text{-}M\!\left(L_t^{(m)}\right),
$$

with `M` fixed across runs for comparability. This frontier is not a concession to weakness; it is the deployable control surface exposed by autoregressive decoding itself. By acting on `\mathcal{F}_t^{(m)}`, UniGround remains a genuine decode-time controller: it reshapes the alternatives currently expressed by the host model, rather than replacing the host decoder with an external reranker or surrogate generator. The same interface also enables portability, because the universal controller needs access only to the host model's active semantic alternatives, not to model-native hidden dynamics.

We compare two routes throughout the paper:

1. **`TLRA_internal`**, the earlier model-coupled path, including `TLRA_zero` and `TLRA_calib`;
2. **`TLRA_univ`**, the universal sidecar path instantiated by UniGround.

This split is methodological rather than cosmetic. `TLRA_internal` measures what can be achieved when model-specific alignment is permitted; `TLRA_univ` measures what remains achievable when strict portability is imposed.

### 4.2 Universal observation space

UniGround is defined over a model-agnostic observation space, but the observation is explicitly **candidate-conditioned**. For image `I`, decoded context sketch `c_t`, candidate span `s`, and a precomputed external region bank `\mathcal{R}(I)=\{r_i\}_{i=1}^N`, we write

$$
\mathcal{O}_{univ}(I, c_t, s)
= \{E_v(I), E_t(c_t), E_t(s), E_v(r_1), \dots, E_v(r_N)\},
$$

where `E_v` is a frozen public vision encoder, `E_t` is a frozen public text encoder, and `c_t = C(y_{<t})` is a deterministic short context sketch extracted from the current decoded prefix. The role of `c_t` is limited: it disambiguates the local generation context, but it is not treated as a globally reliable grounding anchor. This avoids the brittle assumption that an unfinished autoregressive prefix should itself be the dominant visual query.

We denote the resulting external embeddings by

$$
z_I = E_v(I), \qquad z_c = E_t(c_t), \qquad z_s = E_t(s), \qquad Z_R = \{z_{R_i}\}_{i=1}^N.
$$

These variables define the entire learned input to the universal sidecar and constitute the shared semantic substrate on which transfer is meant to hold.

### 4.3 Span proposal through a parameter-free tokenizer bridge

The host model proposes token candidates, but UniGround acts on **candidate spans** rather than raw token IDs. Let `B_m` denote the tokenizer bridge associated with host model `m`. For each candidate token `c \in \mathcal{F}_t^{(m)}`, the bridge produces a normalized span hypothesis

$$
s = B_m(c, y_{<t}),
$$

through four deterministic steps:

1. decode the token or token prefix;
2. canonicalize whitespace and subword markers;
3. expand to a short span hypothesis when the prefix permits; and
4. merge equivalent strings under normalization.

The resulting span set is

$$
\mathcal{S}_t^{(m)} = \{ B_m(c, y_{<t}) \mid c \in \mathcal{F}_t^{(m)} \}.
$$

This bridge is deliberately parameter-free. It may be tokenizer-aware, but it is not allowed to introduce learned host-specific parameters. Its role is not merely syntactic conversion. It is the mechanism that turns model-specific token proposals into cross-model semantic propositions, thereby making a universal controller possible without learning a separate adapter for each vocabulary.

### 4.4 Candidate-driven visual resonance

`Psi_univ` is the only learned component in the universal route, but before it scores a candidate, UniGround first constructs a **candidate-conditioned evidence summary**. Given a candidate span `s`, the external controller uses `z_s` to wake up locally relevant regions from the bank `Z_R`:

$$
a_{t,i}(s)
:=
\frac{\exp(\cos(z_s, z_{R_i}) / \tau_r)}
{\sum_{j=1}^{N} \exp(\cos(z_s, z_{R_j}) / \tau_r)},
$$

and forms the awakened evidence summary

$$
\bar z_R(s) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} a_{t,i}(s)\, z_{R_i}.
$$

This step is the heart of the resonance story. The candidate acts as a semantic query; the image does not answer with a single global vector, but through the subset of external evidence that the candidate can actually wake up. Because the aggregation is conditioned on the same candidate throughout, UniGround avoids the unstable `max-max` spatial substitution in which unrelated regions can dominate the score before and after candidate insertion.

Given this awakened observation, `Psi_univ` predicts

$$
\mathbf{q}_t(s) = \Psi_{univ}(z_I, \bar z_R(s), z_c, z_s)
:= \big[\sigma_{sup}(s), \sigma_{con}(s), \sigma_{abs}(s)\big],
$$

where `\sigma_{sup}` measures visual support, `\sigma_{con}` measures contradiction, and `\sigma_{abs}` measures abstention under uncertainty. UniGround composes them into a single **grounding-resonance energy**

$$
\mathcal{R}_t(s)
:= g_t(s)\,\big(1-\sigma_{abs}(s)\big)\,
\big(\sigma_{sup}(s) - \lambda_{con}\sigma_{con}(s)\big),
$$

where `g_t(s) \in [0,1]` is a deterministic structural gate and `\lambda_{con} > 0` weights contradiction against support.

### 4.5 Risk-aware activation

The universal route should not wake up external evidence for every token. It should activate only when a visually groundable candidate is both relevant and risky. We therefore replace pure entropy gating with a **risk-aware activation score** over object-like candidates:

$$
\rho_t(s)
:=
\pi_t^{host}(s)\,\bigl(1-q_t^{coarse}(s)\bigr),
$$

where `\pi_t^{host}(s)` is the host-side prior mass on span `s`, and `q_t^{coarse}(s)` is a cheap coarse external agreement score computed from the global image embedding and the candidate-conditioned region bank. Intuitively, `\rho_t(s)` becomes large when the host strongly prefers a visually groundable candidate but the external evidence remains weak or mismatched.

The controller is activated when

$$
\Gamma_t
:=
\mathbf{1}\!\left[
\max_{s \in \mathcal{S}_{t,obj}^{(m)}} \rho_t(s) > \tau_{risk}
\right],
$$

where `\mathcal{S}_{t,obj}^{(m)}` is the visually groundable object-candidate subset produced by the bridge and structural gate. This design is intentionally asymmetric: low-entropy but visually risky candidates are allowed to trigger control, whereas high-entropy function-word competition is ignored.

### 4.6 Conservative redistribution and abstention-triggered back-off

Once the controller is active, UniGround applies a bounded, conservative bias rather than claiming to regenerate the host distribution from scratch:

$$
b_t(s) = \alpha \, \Gamma_t \, \tanh\!\big(\mathcal{R}_t(s) / \tau_b\big),
$$

$$
\Delta L_t^{(m)}(v)
:=
\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}_t^{(m)}} A_m(v,s)\, b_t(s),
$$

$$
\widetilde{L}_t^{(m)}(v) = L_t^{(m)}(v) + \Delta L_t^{(m)}(v).
$$

Here `A_m(v,s)` is the deterministic attribution map induced by the tokenizer bridge, `\alpha` controls intervention strength, and `\tau_b` bounds the effective bias. The mechanism is therefore corrective rather than substitutive: UniGround only nudges the host model's currently expressed frontier, and only through a bounded, candidate-aware redistribution.

Abstention remains a first-class safety mechanism. If the controller predicts high uncertainty over the active candidate set,

$$
\max_{s \in \mathcal{S}_t^{(m)}} \sigma_{abs}(s) \ge \tau_{abs},
$$

UniGround backs off and leaves the host logits unchanged. Abstention is therefore not a passive score attached after the fact; it directly governs whether decode-time intervention is permitted to act.

### 4.7 Why the mechanism remains universal

The universality claim rests on a clean separation between semantic control and host-specific realization. `Psi_univ` operates entirely in the external space defined by frozen public encoders; `B_m` and `A_m` are parameter-free attachment rules; and the host model contributes only its active frontier and final decoding. No learned per-model adapter is introduced anywhere in the path from observation to intervention.

This yields a sharper scientific object than model-coupled calibration. The learned parameters define a reusable grounding controller, while tokenizer-specific details remain confined to deterministic interface logic. Put differently, the learned content of the method resides in semantic judgment, not in model-specific attachment.

### 4.8 Controls and methodological scope

UniGround must be distinguished from two simpler alternatives. The first is an **external global prior**, which applies a frozen image-level bias without candidate-aware span verification. The second is the earlier internal route, which includes `TLRA_zero`, `TLRA_calib`, and `Base + LoRA` as model-coupled controls. These comparisons are necessary because they disentangle three different sources of gain: global visual prior, universal candidate-aware control, and model-specific internal alignment.

The central claim of UniGround is therefore precise rather than expansive. It does not assert relation-level omniscience, zero-cost intervention, perfect tokenizer invariance, OCR specialization without dedicated evidence, or universal dominance over all model-coupled controls. Instead, it asserts the following:

> A single learned sidecar, defined in a shared external semantic space and attached through parameter-free model interfaces, can exert useful decode-time grounding control across heterogeneous MLLM families without model-specific retraining.

## 5. Evaluation Protocol

### 5.1 Universal hot-swap validity

The first hypothesis, `H1`, is the core universality claim: the **same** `Psi_univ` checkpoint should transfer across distinct MLLM families without learned adaptation. The evaluation contract is therefore strict:

- no per-model retraining;
- no learned per-model adapter; and
- only parameter-free tokenizer or interface logic may vary.

Any violation collapses the universal claim, regardless of empirical gains on an individual host model.

### 5.2 Module-level validation of `Psi_univ`

The second hypothesis, `H2`, concerns **module validity**. Before asking whether the full system beats Base, the paper must establish that `Psi_univ` itself learns meaningful support/contradiction/abstention judgments in the shared external semantic space. The module table therefore compares:

- `Psi_univ`
- `Hardcoded_Scorer`
- `Zero_Scorer`

Mandatory outputs include macro-F1, class-wise accuracy, abstention behavior, and checkpoint transfer metadata. This table is not auxiliary: it distinguishes `the learned module failed` from `the full system failed to use the learned module effectively.`

### 5.3 System-level hallucination reduction

The third hypothesis, `H3`, concerns mitigation efficacy under that strict portability contract. The main system-level benchmark compares:

- Base
- `VCD`
- `DoLa`
- `TLRA_internal_zero`
- `TLRA_internal_calib`
- `TLRA_univ`

Mandatory outputs include hallucination metrics, length preservation, intervention coverage, latency, and memory overhead. The central reading of this table is not whether `TLRA_univ` dominates every baseline on every model, but whether it delivers consistent grounded gains while preserving its universal attachment contract.

### 5.4 Structure preservation and reasoning retention

The fourth hypothesis, `H4`, asks whether decode-time grounding control remains usable once language quality and general capability are taken into account. To test this, we compare:

- Base
- `TLRA_univ`
- `TLRA_univ_no_gate`
- `TLRA_internal_calib`

Mandatory diagnostics include function-word corruption, continuation stability, reasoning retention, and behavior on OCR-heavy settings where abstention may be preferable to aggressive intervention. Improvements in hallucination scores should not be counted as meaningful if they are obtained by damaging syntax, over-shortening responses, or degrading non-hallucination reasoning behavior.

### 5.5 Portability versus internal alignment ceiling

The fifth hypothesis, `H5`, is a fairness analysis rather than a dominance claim. If `TLRA_internal_calib` achieves stronger results on a particular host model, while `TLRA_univ` transfers across models without retraining, the paper must report that tradeoff directly:

- the internal route may offer a higher task-specific ceiling;
- the universal route offers stronger portability and a broader scientific claim.

The benchmark must not collapse these objectives into a single, misleading notion of superiority. A model-coupled gain is evidence for the value of internal alignment, not evidence against universality; conversely, portability alone is insufficient if the universal route fails to deliver useful grounded control.

### 5.6 Comparison against external global priors

The sixth hypothesis, `H6`, asks whether candidate-aware local control is genuinely necessary. UniGround must therefore be compared against a simpler family of alternatives: frozen image-level presence priors applied as static logit biases throughout generation. This comparison is mandatory because it tests whether candidate-aware span verification provides benefit beyond a global visual prior without local decoding logic.

The benchmark therefore requires:

- `External_Global_Prior`: a frozen global image prior with no candidate-local scoring;
- `TLRA_univ_global_only`: a UniGround variant restricted to global visual evidence; and
- `TLRA_univ`: the full candidate-aware variant.

If `TLRA_univ` fails to outperform these simpler global alternatives, the candidate-aware grounding-control claim must be weakened accordingly. In that case, the paper may still report a universal control framework, but not a strong case for token-local span verification as the source of the gain.

### 5.7 Required ablations

The following ablations are mandatory because each removes a specific component of the claimed mechanism:

1. `TLRA_univ_no_gate`
2. `TLRA_univ_global_only`
3. `TLRA_univ_region_only`
4. `TLRA_univ_no_abstain`
5. `External_Global_Prior`
6. `TLRA_internal_calib`
7. `Base + LoRA` for internal-route fairness only

These ablations serve distinct purposes: `TLRA_univ_no_gate` tests whether structure protection is necessary, `TLRA_univ_no_abstain` tests whether safety-oriented back-off is doing substantive work, `TLRA_univ_global_only` and `External_Global_Prior` isolate the value of candidate-aware control, and `TLRA_internal_calib` plus `Base + LoRA` anchor the portability-ceiling comparison against model-coupled alternatives.

### 5.8 Failure-boundary audits

The final hypothesis, `H7`, concerns failure boundaries and operational cost. The universal claim requires explicit auditing of:

- the Top-`M` ceiling;
- tokenizer-dependent prefix ambiguity;
- collapse of multiple tokenizations to the same normalized span;
- suffix and continuation stability;
- abstention frequency under weak visual evidence;
- abort-trigger frequency and post-abort grammatical survival; and
- latency decomposition across candidate construction, sidecar scoring, logit redistribution, and runtime jitter.

These audits are not auxiliary diagnostics. They determine whether the reported gains are scientifically interpretable, whether the mechanism behaves safely when evidence is insufficient, and whether the portability claim is practical under real decode-time constraints.

## 7. Discussion and Scope of Claims

The principal scientific contribution of UniGround is not that a universal controller must dominate every model-coupled method on every host model. Rather, it is that decode-time grounding control can be externalized into a shared semantic space and still remain operationally useful across heterogeneous MLLM families. The discussion must therefore interpret the empirical results through two coupled lenses: whether the controller is genuinely portable, and what level of grounded benefit it delivers under that portability constraint.

### 7.1 When the universal claim is warranted

A strong universal claim is warranted only when four conditions hold simultaneously. First, the same `Psi_univ` checkpoint must be reused across multiple host models. Second, no learned per-model adapter may be introduced. Third, the universal route must improve grounding quality, or abstain often enough in uncertain cases, to justify its computational footprint. Fourth, when the paper claims candidate-aware local control, the full method must materially outperform simpler external global-prior alternatives. Without this combination of portability, utility, and comparative necessity, the contribution should be stated more narrowly.

### 7.2 Portability and ceiling should not be collapsed

If `TLRA_internal_calib` achieves stronger results on a particular host model, this should not be treated as a failure of the universal formulation. It instead reveals the expected portability-ceiling tradeoff: model-coupled calibration may enjoy a higher host-specific ceiling because it is permitted to exploit internal geometry, whereas UniGround pursues a broader and more transferable control interface. The paper should therefore report the tradeoff directly rather than forcing both routes into a single notion of superiority.

In this regime, the contribution contracts to a more precise statement: UniGround provides a principled universality framework, an explicit portability-ceiling analysis, and a reusable decode-time controller that remains useful even when a model-coupled internal method retains a higher per-host upper bound.

### 7.3 When the claim must contract further

Two outcomes require stronger claim contraction. If the full universal controller is matched by `External_Global_Prior` or by `TLRA_univ_global_only`, then the candidate-aware resonance story is not yet justified as the source of the gain; the paper should then emphasize universal control through frozen external evidence, but not a strong token-local claim. If the method requires learned per-model adaptation at attachment time, the strict universality claim fails altogether and must be withdrawn.

These contraction rules are not rhetorical safeguards appended after the fact. They are part of the scientific framing of the paper itself: the method is designed to make the universal claim falsifiable, and the paper should preserve that falsifiability in how it interprets the final results.
