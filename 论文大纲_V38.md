# Token-Local Resonance Anchoring: Structure-Preserving Logits Intervention for Multimodal Hallucination Mitigation

## Abstract

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) often hallucinate when strong language priors overpower the localized visual evidence required for the next decoding step. Existing inference-time mitigation strategies such as contrastive decoding, attention control, and layer-contrast methods are important competitive baselines, but they do not directly provide a general framework for injecting **token-local visual evidence** into decode-time logits adjustment while preserving language structure. We present **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**, a decode-time intervention framework that reweights candidate tokens using localized support extracted from contextualized visual states.

TLRA is built around three principles. First, **token-local evidence reweighting** replaces coarse correction signals with candidate-wise support computed from localized visual states. Second, **Adaptive Top-$k$ resonance** stabilizes local evidence extraction under a bounded candidate window, making the method compatible with dense grounding scenarios such as OCR-heavy documents and small-object recognition. Third, **Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM)** protects functional syntax and multi-token entities via deterministic root-token lookup and BPE continuation inheritance. To keep comparisons honest, we explicitly separate a strict zero-shot probe (`TLRA_zero`) from a lightweight calibrated variant (`TLRA_calib`).

Rather than claiming premature state-of-the-art performance, we organize the paper around a strict experimental contract: a zero-shot semantic-validity pilot, three evidence chains covering hallucination reduction, structure and reasoning preservation, and local-evidence value, plus explicit efficiency and failure-boundary audits. The resulting paper is not a broad critique of external baselines; it is a positive, falsifiable proposal for structure-preserving decode-time visual control in image-based MLLMs.

## 1. Introduction

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) are highly capable, but their decoding behavior remains vulnerable to hallucination: the model may generate text that is fluent and plausible yet weakly supported by the actual image input. This failure is especially damaging in dense grounding scenarios such as OCR-heavy documents, small-object recognition, GUI understanding, and document reasoning, where the relevant evidence is often local rather than global.

Current inference-time mitigation strategies typically intervene in one of three ways. Contrastive decoding methods such as `VCD` compare different input conditions to reshape the next-token distribution. Attention-based control methods such as `OPERA` monitor abnormal generation patterns and penalize them during decoding. Layer-contrast methods such as `DoLa` exploit differences between shallow and deep model distributions. These are all legitimate competitive baselines, but none of them by themselves define a direct answer to the following question:

**How can we inject token-local visual evidence into decode-time logits adjustment without damaging language structure, multi-token entities, or downstream reasoning ability?**

That question is the center of this paper. We no longer rely on claiming that mainstream baselines share one mistaken mechanism. Instead, we advance a narrower and more defensible positive hypothesis: a decode-time framework that scores candidate tokens using localized visual support can reduce hallucination more faithfully than purely language-side heuristics, provided that it also includes explicit structure-preserving safeguards.

Executing this idea is nontrivial for four reasons. First, **embedding asymmetry**: visual states and output logits do not naturally live in the same space. Second, **candidate limitation**: post-hoc logits intervention can only reweight candidates that the base model already keeps alive. Third, **structural collapse risk**: naive suppression can damage function words, syntax, and long entities. Fourth, **system cost**: local support extraction can become expensive at high visual token counts.

TLRA therefore centers on three design commitments:

1. **Decode-time token-local logits intervention** rather than model retraining.
2. **Adaptive Top-$k$ resonance** as the main local evidence extractor.
3. **VASM** as the structure-preserving protection layer.

To prevent future drift, this benchmark deliberately constrains scope in two ways. First, the main claims are **image-only**; video is not part of the core scientific contract. Second, `TLRA_zero` is treated as a **viability probe**, not as an identity claim the paper must defend at all costs. If it fails the semantic-validity pilot, the paper will contract its main conclusion toward `TLRA_calib` rather than inventing new excuses.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Decode-Time Hallucination Mitigation

Inference-time mitigation for MLLM hallucination has developed along several lines. Contrastive methods such as `VCD` compare logits produced under different visual conditions. Attention-based methods such as `OPERA` detect and penalize pathological generation behavior during the autoregressive process. Layer-contrast methods such as `DoLa` compare distributions from different network depths. These methods demonstrate that hallucination can be mitigated at inference time, but they rely on different intervention signals and involve different latency, robustness, and implementation trade-offs.

### 2.2 Local Evidence vs. Coarse Control Signals

High-resolution MLLMs no longer operate over a single compact visual descriptor. They operate over large spatial token fields. For dense grounding tasks, the useful evidence for a word is often concentrated in a small region of the image. This makes token-local evidence attractive: if the next token is visually supported, the support is often sparse, local, and candidate-dependent. TLRA is built around the claim that decode-time hallucination mitigation should take that locality seriously.

This is not a sweeping claim about all existing baselines. It is a narrower methodological claim: when the task depends on local visual evidence, a framework that explicitly reweights candidate logits using localized support is worth testing against coarser internal alternatives. That claim is experimentally verifiable through internal ablations such as `TLRA_zero` vs. `TLRA_MeanPool`.

### 2.3 Structure Preservation in Decoding

A recurring difficulty in token-level intervention is that not all tokens should be treated equally. Functional syntax, discourse markers, and continuation fragments play a structural role that differs from semantically grounded object words or OCR-bearing entities. A practical decode-time method must therefore distinguish hallucination suppression from indiscriminate token suppression. TLRA addresses this through a deterministic structural mask rather than entropy-only heuristics.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Fairness Boundary: `TLRA_zero` vs. `TLRA_calib`

The first design principle of TLRA is fairness transparency. If the method requires a learned projector to align visual states with lexical representations, it should not be reported as though it were purely zero-shot. We therefore explicitly separate TLRA into two tracks:

1. **`TLRA_zero`**: a strict decode-time probe where the model-native readout is used directly on contextualized final-layer visual states.
2. **`TLRA_calib`**: a lightweight calibrated variant where a small projection `Phi_calib` is trained to improve visual-to-lexical alignment while the base MLLM remains frozen.

This distinction is central to the paper's credibility. `TLRA_zero` is the fair comparison against zero-shot decoding baselines; `TLRA_calib` is the calibrated upper bound. If the semantic-validity pilot shows that `TLRA_zero` is effectively noise, the main paper should pivot to the scientifically useful conclusion that lightweight calibration is the minimum requirement for bridging embedding asymmetry in this family of models.

### 3.2 Bounded Candidate Filtering and Adaptive Top-$k$ Resonance

At decoding step $t$, TLRA first restricts intervention to a bounded candidate set:

$$
\mathcal{C}_t = \text{Top-}M(L_{orig}),
$$

where $M \ll |V|$ is a small candidate window derived from the original logits.

Let $h_L^{(v_j)}$ denote the contextualized visual states from the final layer, and let the visual token volume be

$$
N_v = H \times W.
$$

For each candidate $c \in \mathcal{C}_t$, TLRA computes candidate-wise local support using an adaptive Top-$k$ rule:

$$
k = \max\Big(k_{min}, \lceil \rho \cdot N_v \rceil\Big),
$$

followed by

$$
S_{raw}(c) = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{j \in \text{Top-}k}\frac{score(v_j, c)}{\tau_{sim}}.
$$

Here, `score(v_j, c)` is the patch-level lexical support for candidate `c`, obtained either directly from the model-native readout in `TLRA_zero` or from the calibrated projection in `TLRA_calib`. `tau_sim` is a temperature parameter controlling resonance sharpness.

This design preserves locality while reducing the noise sensitivity of pure max matching. It also makes the paper's claim narrower and cleaner: we are not claiming to solve all multimodal hallucination, but only to test whether candidate-wise localized evidence is more faithful than coarse aggregation on image-based dense grounding tasks.

### 3.3 Relative Resonance Penalty

To convert raw local support into a stable logits intervention, TLRA computes a relative evidence score over the bounded candidate set:

$$
\hat S(c) = \text{Softmax}\big(S_{raw}(c) \mid c \in \mathcal{C}_t\big).
$$

The final candidate logits are adjusted as

$$
L_{final}(c) = L_{orig}(c) - \alpha \cdot \gamma(c) \cdot (1 - \hat S(c)).
$$

This formulation emphasizes suppression of weakly supported candidates relative to stronger local competitors, rather than introducing a separate reward branch that can destabilize decoding.

### 3.4 Structure Preservation via Deterministic VASM

A naive token-level intervention can easily suppress useful structure together with hallucinations. TLRA therefore introduces **Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM)** as a deterministic protection mechanism:

- `gamma(c) = 1` for tokens eligible for visual grounding intervention.
- `gamma(c) = 0` for tokens that should remain structurally protected.

At benchmark level, VASM operates in two stages:

1. **Root-token lookup:** a precomputed lexical table assigns intervention eligibility to visually grounded token groups while excluding function words and other structurally sensitive tokens.
2. **BPE continuation inheritance:** continuation fragments inherit the mask value of their root token instead of being treated independently.

This is important for OCR strings, long entity names, and tokenizer-dependent subword splits. The key point is not that VASM solves every ambiguity problem; it is that TLRA without VASM should be measurably worse on reasoning- and structure-sensitive benchmarks.

### 3.5 What TLRA Does Not Claim

This benchmark deliberately avoids several overclaims:

- `TLRA_zero` is not assumed to work equally well on all architectures.
- `TLRA_calib` is not presented as "still zero-shot."
- VASM is not claimed to solve all polysemy or context sensitivity.
- TLRA is not presented as a universal replacement for `VCD`, `OPERA`, or `DoLa`.
- The main claims do **not** include video.
- TLRA cannot recover a correct token if that token is absent from the initial Top-$M$ candidate set.

These boundaries are part of the paper's honesty, not a weakness to hide.

The key claim here is narrow but central: on tasks that depend on local visual evidence, adaptive local reweighting should be more faithful than coarse aggregation. If `TLRA_AdaptiveTopK` does not clearly beat `TLRA_MeanPool`, the local-evidence thesis should be weakened.

### 4.5 Efficiency and Failure-Boundary Audits

Before any strong practicality claim, TLRA must also report:

- `TPOT` / `ITL`
- `tokens_per_second`
- `peak_vram`
- latency scaling against increasing $N_v$
- sensitivity over `alpha`, `rho`, `k_min`, and `M`
- explicit out-of-candidate failure cases

These audits define whether the method is merely conceptually interesting or actually usable. Failure-case analysis is especially important: the paper should show examples where the correct visually grounded token lies outside the initial Top-$M$ window and is therefore unrecoverable by any post-hoc reweighting strategy of this kind.

## 5. Discussion and Limitations

The strongest version of TLRA is not the one with the biggest narrative, but the one with the cleanest scientific contract:

- it is a decode-time method
- it uses token-local visual evidence
- it preserves structure through VASM
- it separates zero-shot probing from calibrated upper bounds
- it is judged through effectiveness, side effects, local-evidence value, and efficiency

The main unresolved risks remain:

1. **Alignment risk:** `TLRA_zero` may fail if final-layer visual states do not align meaningfully with lexical readout.
2. **Structure risk:** VASM may still miss difficult contextual or tokenizer-specific edge cases.
3. **Efficiency risk:** Adaptive Top-$k$ must prove itself under realistic high-resolution token loads.
4. **Capability boundary:** the benchmark focuses primarily on object- and OCR-heavy dense grounding, not the full space of multimodal errors such as every attribute or relational hallucination.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Overcoming Post-Hoc Entanglement: `BRA_zero` vs `BRA_calib`

A fundamental vulnerability in decode-time visual intervention is embedding space asymmetry. The LLM's output vocabulary matrix ($W_{vocab}$) resides in the terminal logit space, while visual tokens enter at the input layer. To construct a valid local support metric, BRA evaluates the *contextualized* final-layer transformer hidden states. Specifically, we extract the LLM's final hidden states corresponding to the visual sequence, $h_L^{(v_j)} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{vision}}$, strictly extracted immediately prior to the terminal $W_{vocab}$ projection.

We explicitly acknowledge the gamble of **post-hoc entanglement**: deep self-attention implicitly mixes visual states with prefix context. To handle vocabulary projection cleanly without falsely claiming zero-shot alignment if an architecture inherently destroys spatial fidelity, we define a strict operational boundary governed by a formal **Washout Threshold**:

1. **`BRA_zero`**: A zero-shot variant where the projection $\Phi_{zero}$ is the model's native `lm_head`. We compute local support via $logit^{(v_j)} = W_{vocab} \cdot \text{LayerNorm}(h_L^{(v_j)})$. 
2. **The Washout Threshold**: We formally define spatial washout empirically. If `BRA_zero` degrades the base model's POPE F1 score by $>2\%$ during validation, we declare native spatial fidelity mathematically lost for that specific architecture.
3. **`BRA_calib`**: Upon triggering the washout threshold, we pivot to a lightweight calibrated alignment. $\Phi_{calib}: \mathbb{R}^{D_{vision}} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{D_{llm}}$ is strictly defined as a 2-layer MLP projection ($\approx 4.2$M parameters, appending $<0.01$ TFLOPs per step) to project the visual state prior to $W_{vocab}$. It is trained on a frozen set of 5,000 COCO image-patch to bounding-box label pairs, with the base LLM entirely frozen.

**Strict Semantic Negative Sampling (SAM/IoU Assisted):** We optimize $\Phi_{calib}$ via a contrastive InfoNCE loss. Because raw bounding boxes inherently contain background pixels, we institute a strict sampling logic using an off-the-shelf segmentation mask (SAM) to extract strictly foreground patches. Crucially, to avoid latent semantic poisoning where a geometrically distant patch contains a valid object of the same class, negative patches ($W_{vocab}[c^-]$) are cross-referenced to ensure both an Intersection-over-Union (IoU) $< 0.1$ with the target *and* membership in a differing COCO semantic super-category.

### 3.2 Threshold-Gated Adaptive Top-$k$ Pooling

At decoding step $t$, BRA evaluates spatial support. A naive Softmax over all visual patches would force background patches to sum to 1.0, artificially inflating noise. We apply a threshold-gated Sigmoid activation, allowing irrelevant patches to mathematically abstain:
$$
\tilde{P}^{(v_j)}(c) = \text{Sigmoid}\left(\frac{logit^{(v_j)}[c] - \theta_{noise}}{\tau_{sim}}\right) \cdot \mathbb{I}\Big(logit^{(v_j)}[c] > \theta_{noise}\Big)
$$
To prevent $\theta_{noise}$ from becoming a brittle static hyperparameter, it is dynamically defined per-step as the moving **median** of the activation distribution across the $N_v$ patches. To address the vulnerability of dense images (e.g., a crowded scene where most patches genuinely contain objects, causing the median to spike and wipe out valid evidence), we apply a historical upper-bound clamp $\theta_{max}$ derived from the prefix context. We then apply an Adaptive Top-$k$ rule: $k = \max\Big(k_{min}, \lceil \rho \cdot N_v \rceil\Big)$, aggregating only the top-$k$ most supportive activated patches.

### 3.3 VASM and the Dual-Condition Entropy Gate

The final candidate logits are adjusted by penalizing tokens lacking local visual backing. This penalty is gated by a deterministic semantic mask $\gamma(c) \in \{0,1\}$:
$$
L_{final}(c) = L_{orig}(c) - \alpha \cdot \gamma(c) \cdot (1 - \hat S(c))
$$

VASM prevents structural collapse via a dual-tier protection architecture:
1. **Root Token Expectation:** $\gamma=1$ *only* for an unambiguous dictionary of visual object nouns. Polysemy (e.g., "apple" the fruit vs. the company) is handled via a brute-force superset. Out-of-dictionary terms default to $\gamma=0$, guaranteeing structural syntax safety. 
2. **BPE Continuation Inheritance (Dynamic):** Any subword token marked as a continuation fragment inherits the exact $\gamma$ weight of its root token. Raw byte fallbacks inherit the $\gamma$ of the immediately preceding structural subword.

**The Dual-Condition Entropy Gate:** 
Modern LLMs suffer from the "arrogance of language priors," often hallucinating objects with extreme confidence. Therefore, a static confidence threshold that bypasses intervention when confidence is high creates a paradoxical blindspot. We correct this via a dual-condition trigger:
*   **Condition 1 (Confronting Arrogance):** If the model exhibits *extreme* confidence ($Entropy < H_{low}$) AND the candidate token is a visual noun ($\gamma=1$), intervention is **forced**. High confidence on a visual noun *invites* visual scrutiny.
*   **Condition 2 (Uncertainty Support):** If the model generation falls into a borderline uncertainty band ($H_{low} \le Entropy \le H_{high}$), intervention is triggered to provide local visual guidance.
If the token is non-visual ($\gamma=0$) and highly confident, intervention is safely bypassed, preserving batched latency. Candidate sets are strictly bounded to $\mathcal{C}_t = \text{Top-}M(L_{orig})$ where $M \ll |V|$.

## 5. Discussion and Limitations

The strength of Bounded Region Alignment (BRA) lies in its structurally bounded proposition: intervening at decode-time using strictly token-local visual evidence while mathematically protecting language integrity via VASM. However, its execution is governed by severe theoretical and operational constraints:

1. **The Post-Hoc Entanglement Gamble:** The assumption that localized visual support survives deep self-attention in final layers is precarious. If the defined Washout Threshold is crossed (e.g., $>2\%$ POPE F1 degradation), the premise of extracting zero-shot local states must rely entirely on the $\Phi_{calib}$ track, shifting the method from pure zero-shot inference to hybrid alignment.
2. **Domain Shift and `BRA_calib` Overfitting:** Training $\Phi_{calib}$ on 5,000 COCO bounding boxes risks severe overfitting. If tested on fundamentally different domains (e.g., DocVQA, MMMU charts), the calibrated projection might aggressively suppress non-COCO elements.
3. **VASM’s Polysemy False Positives:** Operating as a brute-force superset for polysemy (e.g., penalizing "bank" or "apple" even in abstract contexts) during partial generation context is an inherent limitation. Furthermore, if a valid visual noun is out-of-vocabulary (OOV), it receives $\gamma=0$, rendering BRA completely inactive.
4. **Batched Latency and Memory Bandwidth Trap:** Despite dual-condition entropy-gating and bounding the candidate window to $M$, dynamic BPE mask lookups and moving-median spatial pooling inherently impact VRAM and throughput, severely risking OOM at larger batch sizes (e.g., BS=8).

## 6. Conclusion and Future Execution

This paper outlines Bounded Region Alignment (BRA), a rigorously defined framework for mitigating MLLM hallucination through structure-preserving, token-local logits intervention. By mapping embedding asymmetry (`BRA_zero` vs `BRA_calib`), utilizing threshold-gated Adaptive Top-$k$ pooling (anchored by bounded moving medians), engineering robust language protection via VASM BPE inheritance, and enforcing visual scrutiny on arrogant priors via a Dual-Condition Entropy Gate, we establish a technically precise foundation for decode-time visual control. The claims herein are strictly governed by our hypothesis-driven evaluation protocol spanning Hallucination, Structure, and Local Evidence chains, establishing a verifiable roadmap for empirical execution.
