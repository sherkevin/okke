# Bi-directional Resonance Anchoring: Structure-Preserving Token-Local Logits Intervention for Multimodal Hallucination Mitigation

## Abstract

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) often hallucinate when strong language priors overpower the local visual evidence needed for the next decoding step. Existing mitigation strategies have shown that hallucination can be reduced through contrastive decoding, attention control, or inter-layer comparison, but they do not directly provide a general framework for injecting **token-local visual evidence** into decode-time logits adjustment while preserving language structure. We present **Bi-directional Resonance Anchoring (BRA)**, a decode-time intervention framework that operates in the terminal logits space by reweighting candidate tokens using localized visual support.

BRA is built around three principles. First, **token-local evidence reweighting** replaces coarse global correction with candidate-wise support computed from localized visual states. Second, **Bounded Adaptive Top-$k$ Resonance** extracts stable local evidence from high-resolution image or video token fields using a bounded candidate window and resolution-aware Top-$k$ matching. Third, **Probabilistic Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM)** protects functional syntax and multi-token entities, reducing the risk that hallucination suppression degrades grammar, logical structure, or long lexical units. To keep comparisons fair, we explicitly separate a strict zero-shot variant (`BRA_zero`) from a lightweight calibrated upper-bound variant (`BRA_calib`).

Rather than claiming premature state-of-the-art performance, we organize the paper around three evidence chains: hallucination reduction, reasoning and structure preservation, and the value of local evidence over coarse aggregation. These are instantiated as a five-line evaluation protocol spanning `POPE`, `CHAIR`, `MMBench`, `MME`, `MMMU`, `FREAK`, `DocVQA`, and optional video benchmarks. The resulting paper is not a broad critique of existing baselines; it is a positive, falsifiable proposal for structure-preserving decode-time visual evidence control in MLLMs.

## 1. Introduction

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) are highly capable, but their decoding behavior remains vulnerable to hallucination: the model may generate text that is fluent and plausible yet weakly supported by the actual image or video input. This failure is especially damaging in dense grounding scenarios such as OCR-heavy documents, small-object recognition, GUI understanding, and frame-sensitive video reasoning, where the relevant evidence is local rather than global.

Current inference-time mitigation strategies typically intervene in one of three ways. Contrastive decoding methods compare different input conditions to reshape the next-token distribution. Attention-based control methods monitor abnormal generation patterns and penalize them during decoding. Layer-contrast methods exploit differences between shallow and deep model distributions. These families are all important competitive baselines, but none of them by themselves define a general answer to the following question:

**How can we inject token-local visual evidence into decode-time logits adjustment without damaging language structure, multi-token entities, or downstream reasoning ability?**

This question is the actual center of BRA. The paper no longer depends on claiming that mainstream baselines all share one mistaken mechanism. Instead, it advances a more direct and defensible positive hypothesis: a decode-time framework that scores candidate tokens using localized visual support can reduce hallucination more faithfully than purely language-side heuristics, provided that it also includes explicit structure-preserving safeguards.

The challenge is nontrivial for three reasons. First, local evidence must be extracted from very large image or video token fields without exploding compute. Second, local support must be translated into stable logits adjustments rather than noisy token-level oscillation. Third, hallucination suppression can easily overfire, harming syntax, function words, or multi-token visual entities unless the intervention explicitly protects them. These issues together motivate a framework built around local visual evidence, bounded candidate filtering, and structural masking.

BRA therefore centers on three core ideas:

1. **Decode-time token-local logits intervention** rather than training a new model.
2. **Adaptive Top-$k$ resonance** as the main local evidence extractor.
3. **VASM** as the structure-preserving protection layer that keeps the intervention usable.

To keep the paper honest, we also preserve a strict fairness boundary between `BRA_zero` and `BRA_calib`, and we design the experimental story as a test of effectiveness, side effects, and local-evidence value rather than as a debate artifact.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Decode-Time Hallucination Mitigation

Inference-time mitigation for MLLM hallucination has developed along several lines. Contrastive methods such as VCD compare logits produced under different visual conditions. Attention-based methods such as OPERA detect and penalize pathological generation behavior during the autoregressive process. Layer-contrast methods such as DoLa compare distributions from different network depths. These methods demonstrate that hallucination can be mitigated at inference time, but they focus on different intervention signals and induce different latency, robustness, and implementation trade-offs.

### 2.2 Local Evidence vs. Coarse Control Signals

High-resolution MLLMs no longer operate over a single compact visual descriptor. They operate over large token fields that encode spatial or spatio-temporal layout. For dense grounding tasks, the useful evidence for a word is often localized to a small region or a small frame interval. This makes token-local evidence particularly attractive: if the next token is visually supported, the support is often sparse, local, and candidate-dependent. BRA is designed around the claim that decode-time hallucination suppression should take that locality seriously.

This is not a sweeping claim about all existing baselines. It is a narrower methodological claim: when the task depends on local visual evidence, a framework that explicitly reweights candidate logits using localized support is worth testing against coarser alternatives. That claim is experimentally verifiable through internal ablations such as `BRA_zero` vs `BRA_MeanPool`.

### 2.3 Structure Preservation in Decoding

A recurring difficulty in token-level intervention is that not all tokens should be treated equally. Functional syntax, discourse markers, and continuation fragments play a structural role that differs from semantically grounded nouns or verbs. A practical decode-time method must therefore distinguish hallucination suppression from indiscriminate token suppression. BRA addresses this through a probabilistic structural mask rather than through entropy-only heuristics.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Fairness Boundary: `BRA_zero` vs `BRA_calib`

The first design principle of BRA is fairness transparency. If the method requires a learned projector to align visual states with lexical embeddings, it should not be reported as though it were purely zero-shot. We therefore explicitly separate BRA into two tracks:

1. **`BRA_zero`**: a strict decode-time variant where $\Phi$ is the identity mapping or an equivalent model-native projection.
2. **`BRA_calib`**: a lightweight calibrated variant where $\Phi_{calib}$ is trained to improve visual-to-lexical alignment.

This distinction is central to the paper's credibility. `BRA_zero` is the fair comparison against other zero-shot decoding baselines; `BRA_calib` is an upper-bound or alignment-probing variant, not a hidden substitute.

### 3.2 Bounded Candidate Filtering and Adaptive Top-$k$ Resonance

At decoding step $t$, BRA first restricts attention to a bounded candidate set:

$$
\mathcal{C}_t = \text{Top-}M(L_{orig}),
$$

where $M \ll |V|$ is a small candidate window derived from the original logits.

Let $h_L^{(v_j)}$ denote the post-LLM visual states, and let the visual token volume be

$$
N_v = T \times H \times W.
$$

BRA then computes candidate-wise local support using an adaptive Top-$k$ rule:

$$
k = \max\Big(k_{min}, \lceil \rho \cdot (T \times H \times W) \rceil\Big),
$$

followed by

$$
S_{raw}(c) = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{j \in \text{Top-}k}
\frac{\cos\left(W_{vocab}[c], \Phi(h_L^{(v_j)})\right)}{\tau_{sim}}.
$$

This design is intended to preserve locality while reducing the noise sensitivity of pure max matching. In video settings, it also allows the same mechanism to extend over $T \times H \times W$ without requiring a separate language-only decoding rule.

### 3.3 Relative Resonance Penalty

To convert raw local support into a stable logits intervention, BRA computes a relative evidence score over the bounded candidate set:

$$
\hat S(c) = \text{Softmax}\big(S_{raw}(c) \mid c \in \mathcal{C}_t\big).
$$

The final candidate logits are then adjusted as

$$
L_{final}(c) = L_{orig}(c) - \alpha \cdot \mathbb{E}[\gamma(c)] \cdot (1 - \hat S(c)).
$$

This formulation emphasizes suppression of weakly supported candidates relative to stronger local competitors, rather than introducing a separate reward branch that can destabilize decoding.

### 3.4 Structure Preservation via Probabilistic VASM

A naive token-level intervention can easily suppress useful structure together with hallucinations. Predictive entropy is not sufficient protection, because highly confident language-prior hallucinations are often low-entropy. BRA therefore introduces **Probabilistic Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM)**:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\gamma(c)] = \sum_{pos} P(pos \mid c)\cdot \gamma_{pos}.
$$

Here, the expected mask weight depends on corpus-level syntactic role. Tokens with strong semantic grounding relevance receive high intervention weight, while functional syntax receives lower weight.

To preserve multi-token entities, BRA also includes **BPE continuation inheritance**: continuation fragments inherit the structural weight of the root token instead of defaulting to zero. This is important for OCR strings, long entity names, and tokenizer-dependent subword splits.

### 3.5 What BRA Does Not Claim

The benchmark formulation deliberately avoids several overclaims:

- `BRA_zero` is not assumed to work equally well on all architectures.
- `VASM` is not claimed to solve all context-sensitive polysemy.
- Adaptive Top-$k$ is not claimed to be cost-free at arbitrarily large token scales.
- BRA is not presented as a universal replacement for VCD, OPERA, or DoLa.

These boundaries are part of the paper's honesty, not a weakness to hide.

## 4. Experimental Design

The experimental story is organized around three evidence chains and instantiated as five defense lines.

### 4.1 Evidence Chain A: Hallucination Reduction

#### Defense Line 1: Core Hallucination Suppression

**Goal:** test whether BRA reduces hallucination without simply making outputs shorter or more conservative.

- Benchmarks: `POPE`, `CHAIR`
- Baselines: Base, VCD, OPERA, DoLa, `BRA_zero`, `BRA_calib`
- Mandatory metric: `AGL`

This line should establish that any gain is not merely a length-collapse artifact.

### 4.2 Evidence Chain B: Structure and Reasoning Preservation

#### Defense Line 2: General Capability Preservation

**Goal:** verify that hallucination mitigation does not degrade general multimodal understanding.

- Benchmarks: `MMBench V1.1`, `MME`
- Report perception and reasoning behavior where possible

#### Defense Line 5: Reasoning Boundary and VASM Necessity

**Goal:** test whether BRA harms reasoning when language priors are actually useful, and whether `VASM` is necessary to prevent that failure.

- Benchmark: `MMMU (Hard Subset)`
- Mandatory ablation: `BRA` with VASM vs `BRA_no_VASM`

This evidence chain is what turns VASM from a side mechanism into a central scientific contribution.

### 4.3 Evidence Chain C: Value of Local Evidence

#### Defense Line 3: Dense Spatial Grounding

**Goal:** test whether token-local evidence outperforms coarse aggregation on dense grounding-sensitive tasks.

- Benchmarks: `FREAK` (OCR / Position / Counting), `DocVQA`
- Primary ablation: Base vs `BRA_MeanPool` vs `BRA_zero`
- Secondary reference baseline: DoLa
- Required figure: spatial localization or heatmap comparing coarse evidence to BRA Top-$k$

The key claim here is positive and narrow: on tasks that depend on local visual evidence, local reweighting should be more faithful than coarse aggregation.

#### Defense Line 4: Spatio-Temporal Extension

**Goal:** test whether the same local-evidence framework extends to video without collapsing into a purely language-side heuristic.

- Benchmarks: `VIDHALLUC`, `Video-MME`
- Required outputs: temporal histogram of Top-$k$ concentration, `Frame Hit Rate`

Video is useful here not as decoration, but as a test of whether token-local control survives the shift from spatial to spatio-temporal evidence.

### 4.4 Efficiency and Robustness Audits

Before any strong deployment claim, BRA must also report:

- `TPOT` / `ITL`
- `tokens_per_second`
- `peak_vram`
- latency scaling against increasing $N_v$
- hyperparameter sensitivity over $\alpha$ and $\rho$
- cross-architecture robustness where feasible

These audits define whether the method is merely elegant or actually usable.

## 5. Discussion and Limitations

The strongest version of BRA is not the one with the most aggressive narrative, but the one with the cleanest scientific contract:

- it is a decode-time method
- it uses token-local visual evidence
- it preserves structure through VASM
- it separates zero-shot fairness from calibrated upper bounds
- it is judged through effectiveness, side effects, and local-evidence value

The main unresolved risks remain:

1. **Alignment risk:** `BRA_zero` may underperform if final-layer visual states do not align well with lexical space.
2. **Structure risk:** VASM may still fail in unusual contextual or tokenizer-specific edge cases.
3. **Efficiency risk:** adaptive Top-$k$ must prove itself under realistic high-resolution token loads.
4. **Video scope risk:** the image-to-video extension must be demonstrated rather than assumed.

## 6. Revised Claims (Pending Empirical Execution)

Until the protocol is fully executed, the paper should claim only:

- a positive decode-time framework for token-local visual evidence reweighting
- a structure-preserving masking mechanism for safe logits intervention
- a fair zero-shot vs calibrated evaluation split
- a falsifiable evaluation protocol covering effectiveness, side effects, and local-evidence value

It should **not yet** claim universal superiority, architecture-invariant zero-shot success, or negligible system cost.
