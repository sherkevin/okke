# 3 Method

## 3.1 Cross-modal Synergistic Perturbation

To robustly elicit the internal uncertainty of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) without corrupting task-critical semantics, we propose a cross-modal synergistic perturbation strategy. Unlike independent unimodal noise injection, our method explicitly leverages visual-textual cross-attention to strictly preserve fine-grained semantic integrity.

### Saliency-guided Visual Perturbation
Uniform Gaussian blurring risks irreversibly destroying crucial fine-grained details (e.g., small text, specific object attributes) essential for precise Visual Question Answering (VQA). To mitigate this, we introduce a saliency-guided degradation paradigm driven by text-to-image cross-attention. 

Given the original image $I$ and the textual prompt $T$, we first extract the cross-modal attention map $A_{V,T}$ from the LVLM's pre-trained vision-language projector to localize task-relevant regions. We then derive a continuous saliency mask $M \in [0, 1]^{H \times W}$. The perturbed image $I_i$ is generated by applying a spatial-variant Gaussian blur:
$$
I_i = I \odot M + \phi_{\text{vis}}(I, r_i) \odot (1 - M), \tag{1}
$$
where $r_i$ denotes the base blur radius of the $i$-th perturbation ($r_i < r_j$ for $i < j$), $\phi_{\text{vis}}$ is the blurring operation, and $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard product. This mechanism ensures that semantic-heavy focal objects remain crisp while contextual background variations are introduced, strictly maintaining the foundational semantics required for the task.

### Controlled Textual Paraphrasing
Relying on high sampling temperatures ($\tau$) to generate textual perturbations often induces semantic drift or inherent LLM hallucinations. Instead, we constrain the perturbation within a strict semantic equivalence space by utilizing structural paraphrasing techniques (e.g., synonym substitution and syntax tree restructuring) via a constrained LLM prompt:
$$
T_i = \phi_{\text{para}}(T), \tag{2}
$$
where $\phi_{\text{para}}$ generates the $i$-th syntactically diverse but semantically isomorphic query.

### Multi-modal Synchronization
Rather than arbitrarily mapping the blur radius $r_i$ to the LLM temperature $\tau_i$, we construct the paired perturbation set dynamically. By randomly coupling saliency-guided visual perturbations with controlled textual paraphrases, we form a robust multi-modal input ensemble:
$$
\mathcal{S} = \{\langle I_i, T_j\rangle \mid (i, j) \in \mathcal{P}\}, \tag{3}
$$
where $\mathcal{P}$ represents the sampled perturbation combinations. This synergistic interaction forces the LVLM to perform cross-modal alignment under diverse noise distributions without violating the underlying ground-truth semantics.

## 3.2 Efficient Multi-modal Uncertainty Estimation

To quantify the LVLM's epistemic uncertainty while circumventing the prohibitive computational overhead of generating and evaluating $N$ full responses, we propose an early-stopping semantic entropy framework.

For the input ensemble $\mathcal{S}$, the LVLM generates a set of responses. A lightweight natural language inference (NLI) model, rather than an expensive LLM, evaluates bi-directional semantic entailment between the generated responses to partition them into distinct semantic clusters $\{c_k\}_{k=1}^{N_c}$. 

The uncertainty is formalized as the Shannon entropy of the cluster distribution:
$$
U_{\text{LVLM}} = -\sum_{k=1}^{N_c} p(c_k)\log p(c_k), \tag{4}
$$
where the cluster probability $p(c_k)$ is defined as the empirical frequency $p(c_k) = \frac{|c_k|}{N}$. 

To optimize inference cost in multi-modal pipelines, we integrate an adaptive early-stopping criterion. The sampling of perturbations halts dynamically at step $n$ ($n \ll N$) if the marginal change in $U_{\text{LVLM}}$ falls below a predefined threshold $\epsilon$. This ensures computational tractability for real-world LVLM deployments while accurately capturing output divergence.

## 3.3 Hallucination Detection and Metrics

The derived multi-modal uncertainty score $U_{\text{LVLM}}$ serves as a reliable proxy for hallucination detection. Since LVLMs are prone to over-confidence in their generated tokens, high semantic entropy across equivalence perturbations indicates a failure in robust cross-modal grounding (i.e., hallucination).

We formulate hallucination detection as a binary classification task. An output is classified as hallucinatory if $U_{\text{LVLM}} \ge \gamma$, where $\gamma$ is a globally optimized decision threshold. 

To rigorously evaluate detection performance, we employ standard classification metrics. Let $TP$ (True Positive) denote actual hallucinations correctly detected, $TN$ (True Negative) denote non-hallucinations correctly identified, $FP$ (False Positive) denote non-hallucinations misclassified as hallucinations, and $FN$ (False Negative) denote undetected hallucinations. The overall Detection Accuracy is calculated as:
$$
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}. \tag{5}
$$
Furthermore, to provide a threshold-independent evaluation of our uncertainty estimation, we also report the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC) as a primary metric for hallucination detection capability.
