# Token-Local Resonance Anchoring: A Lightweight Parametric Decode-Time Routing Head for Entity-Centric MLLM Grounding

## Abstract

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) frequently hallucinate when strong language priors overpower the localized visual evidence required for grounding physical entities. While zero-shot inference-time mitigations modulate internal model confidence, they operate without explicit spatial alignment. We present **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**, a lightweight, trained late-fusion parametric head that injects token-local visual evidence directly into decode-time logits adjustment. 

By framing TLRA explicitly as a parametric routing mechanism trained on a bounded 50k caption subset, we establish a rigorous comparison against budget-matched prefill-time adaptations (e.g., LoRA) rather than misrepresenting it as a zero-shot technique. TLRA operates on three architectural principles. First, a **calibrated projection head** ($W_{calib}$) maps spatial patches to BPE-level logit biases, navigating a strict static vs. dynamic memory footprint tradeoff that balances Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) against Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT). Second, **Absolute Evidence Scoring** applies an independent Sigmoid activation over bounded candidate windows to penalize visually unsupported tokens without mathematically granting "free passes." Third, **Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM)** protects functional syntax via an offline BPE-level dictionary. To prevent morphological collapse ("Frankenstein words"), VASM incorporates a **Subword Continuation Rule** anchoring only to tokenizer-aware prefixes, explicitly audited via a novel Morphological Error Rate (MER) metric. This work establishes a falsifiable, boundary-aware pre-registered protocol for parameter-efficient decode-time visual routing.

## 1. Introduction

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit remarkable generative capabilities, yet their autoregressive decoding behavior remains vulnerable to hallucination. In fine-grained physical grounding scenarios—such as small-object recognition and document QA (DocVQA)—global image representations often fail to provide the highly localized evidence required to anchor specific generated tokens.

Current inference-time mitigation strategies (e.g., DoLa, VCD) are powerful zero-shot methods that modulate general model confidence via contrastive decoding or layer-wise distribution shifts. However, because they are zero-shot, they cannot introduce new cross-modal alignment pathways. Conversely, standard parametric approaches (like LoRA) operate entirely in the prefill phase, compressing spatial evidence into the global context window without explicitly routing it to specific token generation steps. This leaves a critical gap: **How can we inject fine-grained, token-local visual evidence directly into decode-time logits adjustment using a lightweight trained intervention, without destroying morphological syntax or hitting severe memory bandwidth walls?**

We introduce **Token-Local Resonance Anchoring (TLRA)**. Crucially, TLRA is *not* a zero-shot decoding trick; it is explicitly formulated as a lightweight, late-fusion parametric fine-tuning head. TLRA extracts absolute local spatial support to bias decode-time logits, strictly limited to entity-centric grounding. By training a dedicated mapping from spatial features to lexical logits on a 50k masked-caption subset, TLRA introduces explicit supervision that zero-shot methods lack. To justify its architectural complexity, TLRA must survive a "do-or-die" evaluation: it must strictly outperform a budget-matched, data-matched prefill LoRA adaptation.

To prevent structural collapse, TLRA introduces Vocabulary-Anchored Semantic Masking (VASM), an offline BPE-level dictionary explicitly mapping physical entity synsets to binary subword masks (accounting for tokenizer prefix spaces like `_cat` vs `cat`). To mitigate the risk of morphological text corruption, VASM restricts the intervention to the initial stems of groundable nouns.

To ensure methodological rigor, we structure the evaluation as a falsifiable audit organized into **three evidence chains and five defenses**:
1. **Hallucination Reduction:** Evaluating standard entity grounding (POPE, CHAIR) while strictly auditing morphological syntax preservation via Average Generation Length (AGL) and Morphological Error Rate (MER).
2. **Structure and Reasoning Preservation:** Auditing VASM's protection of general capabilities (MMBench) and evaluating the performance of a budget-matched `Base + LoRA` control.
3. **Local Evidence Value:** Isolating spatial advantage on FREAK and DocVQA against a matched-parameter `Global_Visual_State` uniform pooling control.
4. **Semantic and Geometric Boundaries:** Bounding the method via an Action/Verb stress test (proving VASM's noun-bias limitation) and acknowledging base geometric anisotropy via a `TLRA_zero` sanity check.
5. **Hardware Bandwidth Physics:** A formal evaluation of the static vs. dynamic memory footprint, mapping the tradeoff between Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) and Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT).

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Decode-Time Hallucination Mitigation

Inference-time mitigation for MLLM hallucination has developed along several lines. Contrastive methods such as `VCD` compare logits under different visual conditions. Attention-based methods such as `OPERA` detect abnormal generation patterns during decoding. Layer-contrast methods such as `DoLa` compare distributions from different network depths. These methods demonstrate that hallucination can be mitigated at inference time, but they rely on different intervention signals and carry different robustness and latency trade-offs.

### 2.2 Local Evidence vs. Coarse Internal Control

High-resolution MLLMs operate over large spatial token fields rather than a single compact visual descriptor. For dense grounding tasks, the useful evidence for a word is often concentrated in a small region. This makes token-local evidence attractive: if the next token is visually supported, the support is often sparse, local, and candidate-dependent. TLRA is built around the claim that decode-time hallucination mitigation should explicitly test that locality.

This is not a sweeping claim about all external baselines. It is a narrower methodological claim: when a task depends on local visual evidence, a framework that explicitly reweights candidate logits using localized support should be tested against coarser internal alternatives under matched conditions.

### 2.3 Structure Preservation in Decoding

A recurring difficulty in token-level intervention is that not all tokens should be treated equally. Function words, discourse markers, and continuation fragments play a structural role distinct from visually grounded entity tokens. A practical decode-time method must therefore distinguish hallucination suppression from indiscriminate token suppression. TLRA addresses this via a deterministic structural mask rather than entropy-only heuristics.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Architectural Scope and the Calibrated Late-Fusion Head

TLRA targets standard ViT + MLP projector architectures where spatial provenance is preserved across visual tokens $X_v$. TLRA operates as a lightweight parametric intervention injected at the late-fusion (decode) stage:

**1. `TLRA_calib` (The Parametric Routing Head):** A lightweight linear projection matrix $W_{calib} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times V}$ maps cached spatial visual states of dimension $D$ directly to the lexical vocabulary size $V$. 
- **Training Formulation:** $W_{calib}$ is trained on a bounded subset of 50k conceptual captions. Crucially, the base LLM and Vision Encoder remain entirely frozen. The objective is standard next-token prediction cross-entropy, but the loss is **strictly masked** to only backpropagate on tokens where VASM $\gamma(y_t) = 1$ (physical entities). $\mathcal{L}_{calib} = -\sum_{t} \gamma(y_t) \log P(y_t \mid X_v, Y_{<t})$. This parametric privilege means TLRA must be compared against budget-matched fine-tuning baselines, not just zero-shot methods.

**2. `TLRA_zero` (Representation Sanity Check):** Computes cosine similarity directly between frozen post-projector visual features ($X_v$) and the LLM's discrete text embeddings ($E_{in}$). Because the ViT/MLP features are optimized to align with the LLM's *input* layers rather than its output vocabulary space, this mapping is geometrically incoherent. `TLRA_zero` is included solely as a sanity check to empirically formalize this well-known representation anisotropy, proving that decode-time spatial routing fundamentally requires the learned $W_{calib}$ projection.

### 3.2 The Hardware Bandwidth Tradeoff: Static vs. Dynamic Memory Footprint

At each decoding step $t$, TLRA maps spatial features $X_v$ to lexical biases for a bounded candidate set $\mathcal{C}_t = \text{Top-}M(L_{orig})$. Computing this routing introduces memory bandwidth challenges during autoregressive generation. We define two implementations to balance Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) and Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT):

**A. Static Precomputation (Optimizing TPOT at the cost of TTFT):** During the prefill phase, cached visual states are mapped to the full vocabulary, yielding a static bias matrix $B = X_v \cdot W_{calib}$, where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{N_v \times V}$. While this incurs a one-time FLOP penalty that degrades TTFT, gathering arbitrary token columns $c \in \mathcal{C}_t$ during generation limits the decode-step computation to uncoalesced memory reads from High Bandwidth Memory (HBM).
**B. Dynamic Sliced-Matmul (Optimizing static footprint at the cost of FLOPs):** We dynamically compute the projection *only* for the Top-$M$ candidates at each step: $B_{step} = X_v \cdot W_{calib}[:, \mathcal{C}_t]$. This avoids storing the $N_v \times V$ matrix, reading instead from $W_{calib} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times V}$.

For standard architectures ($D=4096$, $V=32000$, $N_v \approx 3000$), the static footprint of $B$ is roughly 192MB (in fp16). The true bottleneck is not static VRAM capacity, but the HBM bandwidth saturated by uncoalesced gather operations during decoding. Dynamic sliced-matmul trades FLOPs to avoid these uncoalesced reads, making it vital for Ultra-HD architectures where $N_v \gg D$.

### 3.3 Absolute Evidence Anchoring with Logit Clipping

Relying on a Softmax distribution over a local candidate window mathematically risks granting a "free pass" to universally hallucinated tokens. TLRA computes a raw spatial support score and applies an independent Sigmoid activation:
$$
S_{raw}(c) = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{j \in \text{Top-}k} B_{j, c} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat S(c) = \text{Clamp}\Big(\sigma\big(S_{raw}(c) \cdot \beta\big), 0, 1\Big)
$$
This absolute score is used to apply a clipped penalty, boundedly suppressing tokens lacking spatial support without destroying high-confidence syntactic distributions ($\Delta_L$):
$$
L_{final}(c) = L_{orig}(c) - \alpha \cdot \gamma(c) \cdot \min(\Delta_L, \delta_{max}) \cdot (1 - \hat S(c))
$$

### 3.4 VASM, Tokenizer Leakiness, and the Subword Continuation Rule

To preserve syntax, TLRA utilizes VASM, an offline deterministic binary mask ($\gamma \in \{0, 1\}^V$) extracted from English WordNet physical entity synsets. Building VASM requires explicit engineering to handle tokenizer prefix spaces (e.g., `_cat` vs `cat`) and capitalization variants.

**The "Frankenstein" Word Risk:** Multi-token entities (e.g., "refrigerator" $\rightarrow$ `_refrig`, `erator`) pose a severe morphological risk. If TLRA dramatically alters the logit of the prefix `_refrig`, the base model's internal state may fail to naturally output `erator`, resulting in gibberish suffixes. TLRA enforces a **Subword Continuation Rule**: $\gamma$ is set to $1$ *only* for the initial subword stem. By anchoring the semantic routing purely on the prefix, TLRA allows the base LLM to complete the morphological suffix uninterrupted. The success of this mechanism is audited via a Morphological Error Rate (MER) using a dictionary-based spell checker.

## 5. Discussion and Limitations

The viability of Token-Local Resonance Anchoring depends entirely on surviving strict computational, structural, and baseline budget boundaries. By explicitly reframing TLRA as a lightweight, trained parametric head tailored exclusively for entity-centric grounding, we identify the following mathematically rigid limitations:

1. **The Parametric Baseline Mandate:** TLRA is not a zero-shot mechanism. It utilizes 50k domain-specific caption pairs to learn $W_{calib}$. If it fails to outperform a strictly budget-matched, data-matched LoRA adapter operating in the prefill phase, the architectural complexity of decode-time routing is rendered unjustifiable.
2. **The Memory Bandwidth Tradeoff (TTFT vs. TPOT):** Implementing decode-time spatial routing introduces unavoidable hardware friction. Static precomputation front-loads FLOPs (degrading TTFT) and causes uncoalesced HBM reads during generation (degrading TPOT). Dynamic sliced-matmul avoids the static footprint but incurs dynamic FLOP overhead.
3. **The Morphological "Frankenstein" Risk:** By intervening directly in the BPE logit space, we risk disrupting the continuous hidden states required for multi-token word completion. While the Subword Continuation Rule aims to restrict intervention to prefixes, the Morphological Error Rate (MER) audit will definitively bound how safely we can manipulate BPE probabilities without breaking spelling.
4. **Top-M Unrecoverability (The Hijacking CDF):** The intervention is mathematically constrained by the base model's candidate window $\mathcal{C}_t$. If strong language priors marginalize the ground-truth visual token below the Top-$M$ cutoff prior to intervention, our absolute evidence penalty cannot rescue it.
5. **The Noun-Bias of VASM:** By forcing $\gamma(c) = 0$ outside physical entity stems, TLRA is fundamentally blind to action hallucinations (verbs) and relation hallucinations (prepositions). 

Future execution of this pre-registered protocol will definitively confirm whether a trained decode-time routing head can overcome the inherent morphological fragility and HBM bandwidth walls to provide scalable, localized visual grounding.
