# CHIC: Calibrating Hallucinations via Image-first Commitment in Multimodal Large Language Models

## Abstract Draft

### English

Current multimodal large language models remain, in a decisive sense, text-native autoregressive systems: images condition generation, yet rarely function as the first language of admissibility for semantic meaning. We argue that this is the deeper reason multimodal hallucination persists. The central failure is not merely erroneous output tokens, but a more structural event: an unsupported semantic commitment is allowed to enter the autoregressive prefix before it has been sufficiently licensed by the image. Existing mitigation strategies can reduce symptoms, but many do so through auxiliary branches, external verification, attention heuristics, or retrospective correction, leaving the more fundamental problem unresolved: how should semantic commitments be licensed inside the original multimodal forward pass itself? This paper advances an image-first answer. We instantiate this principle with CHIC (Calibrating Hallucinations via Image-first Commitment). Operating as an image-first commitment bottleneck, CHIC is a strictly single-forward, zero-training, host-internal decoding rule built around Image-First Commitment Risk. Rather than repairing outputs after commitment has already formed, CHIC identifies risky commitments by their late formation, weak persistence across multimodal depth, and weak visual-causal participation in the final decision, and suppresses them before they become part of the prefix. In this way, hallucination mitigation is recast as licensing control over semantic commitment, restoring the image as the first language of admissibility and yielding a more compact, theory-driven, and falsifiable account of multimodal grounding.

# 4. Methodology

## 4.1 Problem Setup and Central Thesis

Let `M` denote an open-weight Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM), `I` an input image, and `Q` a textual query. At decoding step `t`, the model produces a next-token distribution

$$
P_t^{(L)}(\cdot) = p_M(\cdot \mid I, Q, y_{<t}),
$$

where `L` denotes the final decoder layer.

Our starting point is the following architectural diagnosis:

> **Current MLLMs are text-native autoregressive systems whose final semantic commitments are often made too late in the decoding stack, after visual evidence has already been compressed and the language prior has become dominant.**

This diagnosis preserves the original **image-first** intuition, but upgrades it from an input-format claim into an algorithmic principle:

> **The image should be the first language of admissibility.**  
> Text may propose a semantic commitment, but a commitment should only be emitted if it has already been licensed by the image before the final language-dominant consolidation stage.

This can be viewed as a competition between two effective paths:

$$
(Q \rightarrow Z \rightarrow Y),
$$

where `Z` denotes question-triggered language prior and autoregressive inertia, and

$$
(I, Q \rightarrow C \rightarrow Y),
$$

where `C` denotes image-authorized semantic commitment. Hallucination arises when the first path dominates and the second path fails to veto an unsupported commitment. IFCB is designed to restore the second path without constructing a separate counterfactual world or an external evidence pipeline.

Therefore, unlike prior work, we do **not**:

- build a text-only or degraded-image counterfactual branch,
- inject external detector priors into the host context,
- decompose hidden states into "pure visual" and "pure textual" parts,
- rely on attention as an explanation signal,
- perform rollback, beam search retrospection, or multi-round verification pipelines.

Instead, we propose a strictly **single-forward**, **zero-training**, **host-internal** algorithm:

## **IFCB: Image-First Commitment Bottleneck**

The method reads a small number of probe layers from the **same multimodal forward pass**, measures whether a candidate token was already stably supported before final commitment, and further checks whether that commitment is still **causally sensitive to the image channel**. A candidate is therefore suppressed only when it is formed by a **late-stage semantic surge** while exhibiting **weak visual participation**.

To keep the method conceptually clean, IFCB is organized into three deterministic parts:

1. **Commitment probe.**  
   Measures whether a candidate token has persistent support across a small probe band before final commitment.

2. **Visual-causal participation probe.**  
   Measures whether the final candidate remains locally sensitive to the visual channel rather than being dominated by the textual pathway.

3. **Commitment bottleneck rule.**  
   Applies a bounded decode-time penalty only when both signals indicate unsupported semantic commitment.

This decomposition plays the same conceptual role that `VEB_UniGround` achieved via scorer/adapter/decision layering, but here the entire mechanism remains inside a single host forward family and requires no learned external verifier.

---

## 4.2 Why Hallucination Is a Commitment Problem, Not a Token Problem

A token is not hallucinated merely because it is visually unsupported in isolation. Hallucination occurs when a token becomes a **semantic commitment**:

1. it introduces or finalizes an object-level claim,
2. it enters the autoregressive prefix,
3. later tokens then expand and self-consistently elaborate that claim.

Thus, the key failure is not local token uncertainty, but **unsupported commitment formation**.

For a candidate token `w`, define its final commitment at step `t` as the event that it wins the final decoding competition under `P_t^{(L)}` and enters the prefix. Once committed, the future decoding process conditions on

$$
(I, Q, y_{<t}, w),
$$

which means an unsupported token can become the semantic anchor of a whole hallucinated continuation.

This directly explains the shared structure of:

- **POPE**: the final binary answer token (`yes`/`no`) is a commitment about queried object existence,
- **CHAIR**: an object noun in caption generation is a commitment about image content.

Hence, a good method should not ask:

> "Can we build a better prior branch?"

It should ask:

> "Did this semantic commitment emerge early enough inside the same multimodal trajectory to be legitimately attributed to the image, rather than to late autoregressive language inertia?"

This is the key difference between IFCB and a generic token-local patch. The unit of analysis is not arbitrary vocabulary mass; it is the moment when the model upgrades a candidate into a semantic commitment that can steer the future prefix.

---

## 4.3 Architectural Insight: Commitment Timing in MLLMs

We make a deliberately weaker and more defensible claim than prior layer-wise methods.

We do **not** claim:

- some middle layer is "pure visual",
- some late layer is "pure textual",
- any single layer has a direct semantic interpretation in isolation.

Instead, we make a **trajectory claim**:

> In grounded generation, support for a visually correct semantic candidate should emerge **persistently** across a band of multimodal layers before the final lexical commitment is made, and the final commitment should remain materially sensitive to the image channel.  
> In hallucinated generation, support for the chosen token tends to be **weak or inconsistent** through that band, rises disproportionately only near the final layers, and depends only weakly on the actual image.

This claim is fundamentally different from hidden-state decomposition:

- it is **intra-forward**, not cross-input,
- it compares **the same token along depth**, not different modalities,
- it uses intermediate readouts only as **commitment probes**, not as standalone truth predictors.

This distinction is critical for theoretical safety. We never interpret an intermediate distribution as the correct answer distribution. We only ask whether the final committed token had already accumulated stable support before the last-stage language consolidation, and whether the final commitment is still genuinely influenced by the image channel.

---

## 4.4 Probe-Layer Readout in a Single Forward Family

Let the decoder have `L` layers. Choose a small probe set

$$
\mathcal P = \{l_1, l_2, \dots, l_m\}, \qquad 1 \le l_1 < \dots < l_m < L,
$$

for example a fixed normalized depth schedule such as

$$
\mathcal P = \left\{\left\lfloor \frac{L}{3} \right\rfloor,\; \left\lfloor \frac{L}{2} \right\rfloor,\; \left\lfloor \frac{2L}{3} \right\rfloor,\; L-1 \right\}.
$$

At decoding step `t`, let `h_t^{(l)}` be the hidden state at layer `l`. For every probe layer and the final layer, we use the same frozen final normalization and LM head to obtain **probe readouts**:

$$
z_t^{(l)} = W \, \mathrm{Norm}(h_t^{(l)}), \qquad l \in \mathcal P \cup \{L\},
$$

with induced probe distributions

$$
P_t^{(l)} = \mathrm{Softmax}(z_t^{(l)}).
$$

### Important interpretation note

This operation is **not** used to claim that `P_t^{(l)}` is a calibrated model of visual truth.  
It is used only to compare **the same candidate token across depth** under the **same multimodal context**. This avoids the two main pathologies of prior work:

1. **No counterfactual OOD branch** is introduced.
2. **No pure-modality decomposition** is assumed.

The probe distributions are therefore trajectory statistics, not independent semantic experts.

---

## 4.5 Final Frontier and Commitment Candidates

At step `t`, define the final plausible frontier as the top-`K` candidate set

$$
\mathcal V_t = \mathrm{TopK}\!\left(P_t^{(L)}\right).
$$

The method only intervenes within this set. This serves two purposes:

1. It keeps the algorithm computationally light.
2. It avoids destabilizing grammatical tail tokens that are irrelevant to the actual decoding choice.

Unlike older truncation heuristics, this frontier is **not** our scientific contribution. It is merely a bounded action surface for a theory whose real core lies in the depth-wise commitment dynamics defined below.

---

## 4.6 Persistent Support and Late Commitment Surge

For any candidate token `w \in \mathcal V_t`, define its **probe-layer support**:

$$
S_t(w) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{l \in \mathcal P} \log P_t^{(l)}(w).
$$

Define its **final commitment score**:

$$
F_t(w) = \log P_t^{(L)}(w).
$$

Then define the **late commitment surge**

$$
U_t(w) = \big(F_t(w) - S_t(w)\big)_+,
$$

where \((x)_+ = \max(0,x)\).

Intuitively:

- if `w` was already stably supported through the probe band, `S_t(w)` is large and `U_t(w)` is small;
- if `w` only becomes strong at the final stage, `U_t(w)` becomes large.

However, late sharpening alone is not enough to identify hallucination, because some correct tokens may also become slightly sharper near the end. We therefore add a **persistence term**.

Define the layer-persistence indicator

$$
\mathbf{1}_l(w) =
\begin{cases}
1, & w \in \mathrm{TopK}\!\left(P_t^{(l)}\right),\\
0, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

and the **persistence ratio**

$$
\Pi_t(w) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{l \in \mathcal P} \mathbf{1}_l(w).
$$

Thus:

- `\Pi_t(w)` high means the token is consistently present across the commitment band,
- `\Pi_t(w)` low means the token is a late entrant that wins only near the final layer.

---

## 4.7 Visual-Causal Participation

Depth persistence alone is not sufficient. A purely language-driven token could in principle display a smooth depth trajectory while still being effectively independent of the image. To preserve the original image-first intuition, IFCB explicitly measures whether the final committed token is causally tied to the visual pathway.

Let `l^*` denote a designated fusion probe layer, for example

$$
l^* = \left\lfloor \frac{L}{2} \right\rfloor.
$$

Let the hidden states at that layer be partitioned by token positions into

$$
H_{vis}^{(l^*)} = \{h_j^{(l^*)} : j \in \mathcal I_{vis}\},
\qquad
H_{txt}^{(l^*)} = \{h_j^{(l^*)} : j \in \mathcal I_{txt}\},
$$

where `\mathcal I_{vis}` and `\mathcal I_{txt}` are the visual-token and text-token index sets of the host serialization.

For a candidate token `w`, define its **visual-causal participation ratio**

$$
G_t(w)
=
\frac{
\left\|
\nabla_{H_{vis}^{(l^*)}} z_t^{(L)}(w)
\right\|_1
}{
\left\|
\nabla_{H_{vis}^{(l^*)}} z_t^{(L)}(w)
\right\|_1
+
\left\|
\nabla_{H_{txt}^{(l^*)}} z_t^{(L)}(w)
\right\|_1
+
\varepsilon
}.
$$

Interpretation:

- `G_t(w)` close to `1` means the final logit of `w` is strongly sensitive to the visual pathway;
- `G_t(w)` close to `0` means the final logit of `w` is dominated by the textual pathway.

This is **not** an attention score and **not** a modality decomposition. It is a local sensitivity statistic of the final commitment with respect to the visual versus textual token groups inside the same forward graph.

In practice, `G_t(w)` is evaluated only for the final top-`K` frontier, so the backward cost remains bounded and localized.

---

## 4.8 Image-First Commitment Risk

We now define the central quantity of IFCB.

For each candidate `w \in \mathcal V_t`, define the **Image-First Commitment Risk**

$$
R_t(w) = U_t(w)\big(1-\Pi_t(w)\big)\big(1-G_t(w)\big).
$$

This quantity is zero in three benign regimes:

1. **No late surge:** `U_t(w)=0`
2. **Persistent support:** `\Pi_t(w)=1`
3. **Strong visual participation:** `G_t(w)=1`

It becomes large exactly when:

- the token gains probability disproportionately late, and
- it was not persistently supported across the multimodal probe band, and
- its final commitment is only weakly coupled to the image.

This is the precise formalization of our image-first principle:

> a semantic token should not be granted final commitment by the language-dominant tail of the network unless it has already earned persistent support earlier in the same multimodal trajectory and the final commitment remains causally tied to the image channel.

---

## 4.9 Image-First Commitment Bottleneck Rule

The decode-time intervention is intentionally minimal.  
For each `w \in \mathcal V_t`, define the calibrated logit:

$$
\tilde z_t(w) = z_t^{(L)}(w) - \alpha R_t(w),
$$

and leave all tokens outside the frontier unchanged:

$$
\tilde z_t(w) = z_t^{(L)}(w), \qquad w \notin \mathcal V_t.
$$

The final decoding distribution is

$$
\tilde P_t = \mathrm{Softmax}(\tilde z_t).
$$

We then decode exactly as the host would normally decode, but using `\tilde P_t`.

### Why this is a bottleneck

This is not a generic logit penalty.  
It is a **commitment bottleneck** because a candidate token may enter the prefix only if:

- it survives the host’s native final competition, and
- it also survives the image-first admissibility test encoded by `R_t(w)`.

Hence the image is reinstated as the first language of admissibility.

---

## 4.10 No Extra Prior World, No External Verifier, No Rollback

The main scientific advantage of IFCB is what it **does not need**.

### 4.10.1 No prior branch

We never construct:

- text-only branch,
- blank-image branch,
- mean-visual branch,
- low-resolution surrogate prior,
- noisy-image counterfactual.

Therefore IFCB avoids the central theoretical weakness shared by VCD-style, DHCP-style, and VLUN-style methods: they all require a second world whose relation to the true forward pass is mathematically ambiguous or empirically OOD.

### 4.10.2 No hidden-state decomposition

We never assert:

$$
h = h_{\text{vis}} + h_{\text{text}}
$$

or any equivalent modal partition.  
IFCB only measures how the *same token* evolves across depth in the *same multimodal forward*.

### 4.10.3 No attention-as-explanation

No attention weights, head maxima, sink masks, or attention windows are used as grounding signals. This avoids the main critiques that defeated OPERA-like and VCD-like internal gating variants. The only image-related term in IFCB is the local gradient-based participation ratio `G_t(w)`, which quantifies whether the final commitment is sensitive to visual tokens, not whether the model happens to attend to them.

### 4.10.4 No rollback or beam search

IFCB never rewinds the prefix.  
It acts before commitment enters the prefix, not after hallucination has already expanded into a chain. In this sense it solves the prefix-contamination problem earlier and more cleanly than rollback-style methods.

## 4.11 Theoretical Assumptions

We now state the assumptions under which the method is justified.

### Assumption A1: Persistent-Grounding Hypothesis

For a visually grounded semantic candidate `w_g`, support should emerge before the final lexical commitment stage, and the final commitment should remain meaningfully image-sensitive. Formally, there exist constants

$$
\rho_g \in (0,1], \qquad \gamma_g \ge 0, \qquad \eta_g \in (0,1]
$$

such that

$$
\Pi_t(w_g) \ge \rho_g,
\qquad
U_t(w_g) \le \gamma_g,
\qquad
G_t(w_g) \ge \eta_g.
$$

### Assumption A2: Late-Surge Hallucination Hypothesis

For a hallucinated candidate `w_h`, the final commitment is created disproportionately late by language inertia and depends only weakly on the actual image. Thus there exist constants

$$
\rho_h \in [0,1), \qquad \gamma_h > 0, \qquad \eta_h \in [0,1)
$$

such that

$$
\Pi_t(w_h) \le \rho_h,
\qquad
U_t(w_h) \ge \gamma_h,
\qquad
G_t(w_h) \le \eta_h.
$$

### Assumption A3: Margin Separability

The host’s final logits for the grounded token and the hallucinated token are close enough that a bounded penalty can reverse the wrong ordering without destroying the rest of the frontier.

This is mild and empirically testable: decode-time methods are only useful where the correct token already lies in the plausible frontier.

---

## 4.12 Main Proposition: Hallucination Appears as a Late, Weakly Visual Commitment Outlier

Under A1 and A2,

$$
R_t(w_g) \le \gamma_g(1-\rho_g)(1-\eta_g),
$$

while

$$
R_t(w_h) \ge \gamma_h(1-\rho_h)(1-\eta_h).
$$

Therefore, if

$$
\gamma_h(1-\rho_h)(1-\eta_h) > \gamma_g(1-\rho_g)(1-\eta_g),
$$

hallucinated candidates incur strictly higher commitment risk than grounded candidates.

### Proof

For grounded tokens:

$$
R_t(w_g)=U_t(w_g)\big(1-\Pi_t(w_g)\big)\big(1-G_t(w_g)\big)
\le \gamma_g(1-\rho_g)(1-\eta_g).
$$

For hallucinated tokens:

$$
R_t(w_h)=U_t(w_h)\big(1-\Pi_t(w_h)\big)\big(1-G_t(w_h)\big)
\ge \gamma_h(1-\rho_h)(1-\eta_h).
$$

If the second lower bound exceeds the first upper bound, the risk scores are ordered strictly. QED.

### Interpretation

This proposition is the mathematical core of IFCB:

> grounded tokens are persistent before commitment and remain image-sensitive, while hallucinated tokens are outliers whose support arrives too late and whose final commitment is only weakly visual.

This is a trajectory statement, not a modality decomposition statement.

---

## 4.13 Decode-Time Correctness Condition

Let `w_h` be the host’s incorrect top candidate and `w_g` the correct grounded candidate.  
After applying IFCB,

$$
\tilde z_t(w_g) > \tilde z_t(w_h)
$$

whenever

$$
z_t^{(L)}(w_g) - z_t^{(L)}(w_h)
>
\alpha \big(R_t(w_g)-R_t(w_h)\big).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\alpha >
\frac{z_t^{(L)}(w_h)-z_t^{(L)}(w_g)}
{R_t(w_h)-R_t(w_g)}
$$

provided `R_t(w_h) > R_t(w_g)`.

### Meaning

This gives a precise sufficient condition for successful correction:

- the hallucinated token must have larger commitment risk than the grounded token,
- and the penalty strength must exceed the host’s wrong logit margin normalized by that risk gap.

This is far more honest and defensible than claiming a universal exact correction theorem.

## 4.14 Why IFCB Is More Defensible Than Prior Families

### Against VCD-style degraded-image prior extraction

VCD-style methods require a second visual world, such as:

- noisy image,
- low-resolution image,
- masked image,
- or some other degraded branch.

Their core weakness is that the degraded branch is neither a clean linguistic prior nor a guaranteed in-distribution visual condition. IFCB avoids this entirely because it never leaves the original multimodal forward. Instead of constructing a second visual world, it asks whether the token's final commitment in the real world is both persistent across depth and locally sensitive to the image channel.

### Against DHCP/VLUN-style causal differential branches

Methods that subtract full-vs-prior logits rely on a second branch whose internal dynamics are not the same as the original branch. Even if the comparison is performed in output space, the baseline world remains theoretically under-motivated. IFCB replaces cross-world comparison with **same-world depth comparison** plus a local **visual-causal participation** term.

### Against attention-gated methods such as OPERA variants

Attention can be low exactly when hallucination is strongest, making attention-based gating self-defeating. IFCB does not use attention to infer grounding. It uses token commitment persistence across depth together with gradient-based visual participation.

### Against rollback/retrospection

Rollback methods detect the problem after hallucination has already entered the prefix. IFCB acts before commitment is emitted, which is both cleaner and cheaper.

### Against external evidence bottlenecks

External verifiers can be effective but easily become engineering-heavy, benchmark-shaped, and difficult to present as a clean algorithmic contribution. IFCB keeps the entire method inside the host model's own multimodal forward family, which is a much more conference-friendly algorithmic story.

## 4.15 Why This Still Realizes the Original “Image as First Language” Intuition

The original intuition was:

> A true multimodal system should treat the image as the first language, rather than using the image as a weak side condition to a text-native decoder.

IFCB realizes this principle in the following precise sense:

1. The final output remains textual.
2. But a semantic candidate is not allowed to become a committed token merely because the final language-dominant layers prefer it.
3. It must first exhibit persistent support inside the earlier multimodal commitment band of the same forward.
4. Its final commitment must remain causally sensitive to the image channel itself.

Therefore, the image becomes the first language of **admissibility**, not necessarily the first language of **surface realization**.

This is stronger and more defensible than saying “we directly output image tokens.”

## 4.16 Benchmark Alignment Without Benchmark-Specific Theory

Although the method is general, the paper-level evaluation should be organized around a small core set of hallucination benchmarks that expose the same failure mechanism under different output surfaces.

This evaluation choice should not be read as a benchmark-specific redesign. We place **POPE**, **CHAIR**, and **HallusionBench** at the center because they are the cleanest measurable probes of a broader phenomenon: **unsupported object-centered semantic commitment**. The method itself is not tied to binary existence questions, caption nouns, or any particular annotation protocol; these benchmarks are simply the settings where the commitment mechanism is easiest to isolate, falsify, and compare against prior work.

### POPE

In POPE, the final `yes/no` answer is the semantic commitment.  
The queried object is already specified in the textual prefix. A wrong `yes` therefore corresponds to a **late binary commitment** unsupported by sufficiently persistent multimodal evidence and only weakly tied to the actual image.

Thus POPE instantiates **binary commitment failure**.

### CHAIR

In CHAIR, an object noun in caption generation is itself the semantic commitment.  
A hallucinated noun is a direct instance of **object commitment failure**, often followed by relational or descriptive continuation.

Thus CHAIR instantiates **generative object commitment failure**.

### HallusionBench

In HallusionBench, the model is forced to answer visually delicate questions whose failure modes are no longer reducible to a single binary existence token or a single caption noun.  
The benchmark therefore tests whether IFCB can still suppress unsupported semantic commitments when the commitment is expressed through broader visual reasoning, attribute discrimination, or visually confusable scene interpretation.

Thus HallusionBench instantiates **fine-grained visual commitment failure**.

These benchmarks therefore test the same abstract mechanism:

> whether unsupported semantic commitments are allowed to enter the autoregressive prefix.

Taken together, `POPE + CHAIR + HallusionBench` cover the three paper-critical surfaces of the same underlying pathology:

- **binary object commitment**,
- **generative object commitment**,
- **fine-grained visual reasoning commitment**.

This is why we can center the paper on them empirically without presenting IFCB as a benchmark-specific patch.

### Practical evaluation tiers for this paper

To keep the paper both publishable and executable, the benchmark protocol should be tiered rather than flat.

#### Must-run benchmarks

1. **POPE** with all three official splits: `random`, `popular`, and `adversarial`.  
   This remains the primary object-hallucination benchmark and the clearest place to test whether IFCB improves commitment decisions under increasing distractor pressure.

2. **CHAIR** on COCO captioning.  
   This verifies that IFCB is not merely improving short yes/no decisions, but can also suppress unsupported object commitments in open-ended generation.

3. **HallusionBench**.  
   This is the key third pillar that prevents the paper from looking like a POPE/CHAIR-only object-hallucination patch. It shows whether the same image-first commitment rule survives in more visually subtle and semantically entangled cases.

#### Strongly recommended benchmarks

1. **MME**.  
   This should be used as the primary capability-retention benchmark because it remains close to visual perception and hallucination-sensitive recognition rather than drifting into a purely general-language score.

2. **MMBench**.  
   This should be used as the broad supplementary generalization benchmark to show that IFCB does not buy hallucination reduction by collapsing overall multimodal competence.

In other words, the intended paper-level matrix is:

- **Main hallucination table:** `POPE + CHAIR + HallusionBench`
- **Strong supplementary/generalization table:** `MME + MMBench`

This tiered protocol is also aligned with the datasets currently confirmed in the project environment, namely the available `POPE` splits, `COCO` annotations needed for `CHAIR`, `HallusionBench_hf`, `MME_hf`, and `MMBench_EN_hf`.

## 4.17 Minimal Engineering Realization

IFCB is intentionally lightweight.

At each decoding step it requires:

1. one standard multimodal forward pass,
2. reading a small fixed set of probe-layer hidden states,
3. applying the same frozen final norm + LM head to those states,
4. computing `R_t(w)` only on the final top-`K` frontier,
5. evaluating the visual participation ratio `G_t(w)` only for that same frontier.

No training is required.  
No auxiliary vision model is required.  
No external verifier is required.  
No branch-specific KV cache is required.  
No host-specific learned attachment is required.

Relative to the host model, the extra cost is dominated by:

- a few extra LM-head projections on selected layers,
- and frontier-level risk computation.

This keeps the method squarely within the algorithmic, zero-training, open-weight regime.

## 4.18 Scope of the Claim

We intentionally make a precise claim, not an inflated one.

We do **not** claim:

- exact recovery of visual causality,
- pure visual/text factorization,
- universal semantic meaning of any single probe layer,
- complete elimination of all hallucinations,
- correctness outside the plausible frontier.

What we do claim is:

> In open-weight MLLMs, unsupported semantic commitments often manifest as candidates whose probability is created disproportionately late within the same multimodal forward pass and whose final commitment is only weakly coupled to the image channel. A decode-time bottleneck based on depth-wise persistence and visual-causal participation can suppress such commitments without counterfactual branches, external verifiers, or model retraining.

This is the actual scientific contribution of IFCB. In particular, the claim is intentionally narrower than that of a universal external verifier framework: IFCB is not a full task-general evidence system, but a theory-first decode-time algorithm for suppressing unsupported commitments in open-weight MLLMs. Its strength is algorithmic concentration rather than external universality.

## 4.19 Testable Predictions

The theory yields several clear empirical predictions.

### Prediction 1

On **POPE**, hallucinated `yes` answers should exhibit:

- larger late commitment surge `U_t`,
- lower persistence `\Pi_t`,
- lower visual participation `G_t`,
- and therefore higher commitment risk

than correct `yes` or correct `no` answers.

### Prediction 2

On **CHAIR**, hallucinated object nouns should show:

- lower persistence `\Pi_t`,
- higher late surge `U_t`,
- lower visual participation `G_t`,
- and therefore higher `R_t`

than grounded nouns.

### Prediction 3

On **HallusionBench**, samples judged incorrect or hallucinatory should exhibit higher average commitment risk on the decisive answer tokens than visually faithful responses to semantically comparable prompts.

### Prediction 4

Compared with:

- VCD-like two-branch contrast,
- OPERA-like attention or rollback control,
- and external verifier pipelines,

IFCB should provide a better trade-off between:

- theoretical cleanliness,
- zero-training deployability,
- and benchmark gains on object-centered hallucination tests.

### Prediction 5

On **MME** and **MMBench**, IFCB should preserve or improve perception-heavy capability scores, showing that image-first commitment control does not merely suppress output aggressively, but regularizes unsupported commitments while retaining usable multimodal competence.

## 4.20 Summary

IFCB reduces hallucination control to a single principle:

> **A token should not be accepted as a semantic commitment unless its support has persisted early enough inside the same multimodal trajectory and its final commitment remains causally tied to the image channel.**

This gives a genuinely theory-first, image-first, zero-training algorithm:

- no fabricated prior world,
- no external semantic bottleneck,
- no hidden-state modality decomposition,
- no attention explanation assumptions,
- no rollback engineering.

In short, IFCB does not ask whether text can be corrected after it hallucinates.  
It asks a stricter question:

> **Did the image authorize this commitment before language inertia finalized it?**

That is the sense in which the image once again becomes the first language of truth.

## 4.21 Positioning Relative to Closest Recent Work

The most relevant neighboring literature is the emerging family of **zero-training, decode-time, host-internal** methods that attempt to diagnose or correct hallucination from runtime signals. These methods differ in the particular symptom they monitor:

- **VCD**-style methods compare the original multimodal forward against a degraded visual condition.
- **OPERA**-style methods detect unstable decoding behavior from internal attention-derived signals and then penalize or retrospectively adjust decoding.
- **DAMO** and related layer-dynamics methods emphasize cross-layer stability and the prevention of late-stage overthinking.
- **DeCo**-style methods inject earlier-layer evidence into the final decoding stage to counteract language-prior dominance.
- **REVIS**-style methods perform training-free latent steering to suppress hallucination-prone directions.
- **DHCP** and **VL-Uncertainty** are primarily detection-oriented, but are still close neighbors because they also infer hallucination from internal runtime behavior rather than from external supervision.

This proximity has an important implication: IFCB should not be presented as novel merely because it reads intermediate layers, uses gradient-derived signals, or intervenes at decode time. Those ingredients are increasingly common. The real distinction of IFCB lies elsewhere.

### Central differentiating claim

IFCB should be understood as a **commitment-theoretic decoding rule** rather than as another signal-specific correction heuristic. Its central object is the Image-First Commitment Risk

$$
R_t(w) = U_t(w)\big(1-\Pi_t(w)\big)\big(1-G_t(w)\big),
$$

which factorizes hallucinated commitment formation into three jointly necessary symptoms:

1. **late commitment surge** `U_t(w)`,
2. **low persistence across the multimodal probe band** `\Pi_t(w)`,
3. **weak visual-causal participation** `G_t(w)`.

This factorization yields a narrower and more defensible claim than broad assertions of visual faithfulness. IFCB does not claim that any one signal alone is sufficient to identify hallucination. Instead, it claims that unsupported semantic commitments become most identifiable when these three symptoms co-occur inside the **same forward trajectory**.

### Why this positioning matters

Most neighboring methods emphasize only one or two aspects of the failure process:

- layer-wise stabilization or semantic trajectory,
- visual-versus-text contribution,
- or test-time correction/intervention.

IFCB is intended to occupy a different point in this design space: it converts these observations into a **single admissibility criterion for semantic commitment**. This is the key reason the paper should frame hallucination as a **commitment failure** rather than as a generic token-local error.

### Practical implication for empirical validation

Because the novelty claim is integrative rather than ingredient-level, the empirical section must test the full factorization directly. In particular, the paper should include:

- single-factor ablations (`U` only, `\Pi` only, `G` only),
- pairwise ablations,
- and the full three-factor rule.

Without such evidence, the three-term risk decomposition may appear rhetorically attractive but scientifically under-identified. By contrast, if the full rule consistently outperforms its reduced variants across `POPE`, `CHAIR`, and `HallusionBench`, then the paper can argue that IFCB contributes not only a new formula but also a sharper unit of analysis for hallucination control.

The expected empirical reading should also be disciplined. Stronger performance on `POPE` alone is not sufficient to support the full claim, because a purely conservative binary suppressor could also improve object-existence accuracy. The claim becomes more persuasive only if the same rule:

- improves **binary commitment control** on `POPE`,
- reduces **generative object commitment errors** on `CHAIR`,
- remains effective under **fine-grained visual reasoning pressure** on `HallusionBench`,
- while preserving acceptable capability scores on `MME` and `MMBench`.

This multi-surface validation is important because IFCB is not proposed as a benchmark-shaped patch for one answer format. It is proposed as a decode-time law for suppressing unsupported commitments across different observable surfaces of the same failure mechanism.

In this sense, IFCB should be positioned not as "the only method that uses internal signals," but as a method that gives those signals a more compact and falsifiable semantic interpretation: **the image acts as the first language of admissibility for semantic commitment**.

## 4.22 Minimal but Sufficient Experimental Program

The empirical section should be designed as a **layered validation package** rather than as an unlimited benchmark sweep. For ACM MM, the goal is not to show that IFCB was tried everywhere, but to show that its commitment-theoretic claim survives across the smallest set of evaluation surfaces that jointly test object hallucination, generative hallucination, fine-grained visual reasoning, and capability retention.

The host-model policy for this paper is now fixed to **two open-weight hosts**:

- `LLaVA-1.5`,
- `InstructBLIP`.

These two hosts should not be treated as interchangeable bookkeeping choices. `LLaVA-1.5` should serve as the **primary development and main-table host**, because it is the more standard and recognizable contemporary open-weight baseline for decode-time intervention studies. `InstructBLIP` should serve as the **cross-architecture validation host**, because it helps show that IFCB is not merely exploiting one particular implementation detail of a single LLaVA-family stack.

### 4.22.1 Main experimental matrix

The paper should be organized around three tables and one small qualitative block.

**Table A: Main hallucination benchmarks**

This is the paper-critical table and should contain:

- `POPE` with `random`, `popular`, and `adversarial`,
- `CHAIR` on COCO captioning,
- `HallusionBench`.

These benchmarks should be reported for **both** selected host models:

- `LLaVA-1.5`,
- `InstructBLIP`.

The mandatory model rows should be:

- `Base` / vanilla decoding,
- `VCD`,
- `DAMO`,
- `IFCB`.

If resources allow one additional same-regime comparison, the preferred fifth row is:

- `DeCo`.

This table is the central evidence for the paper because it directly tests whether IFCB improves hallucination control across the three output surfaces most aligned with the commitment claim.

**Table B: Capability-retention and generalization**

This table should use:

- `MME`,
- `MMBench`.

These should also be reported on both hosts, but under limited budget the paper can prioritize a fuller capability table on `LLaVA-1.5` first and then mirror the essential `Base` vs `IFCB` comparison on `InstructBLIP`.

At minimum, it must report:

- `Base`,
- `IFCB`.

If budget allows, `VCD` and `DAMO` should also be included so that the paper can show not only that IFCB suppresses hallucination, but also that its trade-off against competing decode-time methods remains acceptable on broader multimodal competence.

**Table C: Ablation and mechanism identification**

This table should be run on a representative subset first and then, if stable, on the full main benchmarks. The mandatory rows are:

- `U` only,
- `\Pi` only,
- `G` only,
- `U + \Pi`,
- `U + G`,
- `\Pi + G`,
- full `U(1-\Pi)(1-G)`.

This is the most important non-benchmark table because IFCB's novelty claim is not that any one signal is new, but that the three signals form a more identifiable law of unsupported commitment when combined.

**Qualitative block**

The paper should include a compact qualitative figure showing:

- one `POPE` example,
- one `CHAIR` example,
- one `HallusionBench` example,
- and the corresponding token-level or answer-level `U`, `\Pi`, `G`, and `R` behavior.

This figure should visually support the claim that hallucination is associated with a late commitment surge plus weak persistent and visual support, rather than with generic decoding noise.

### 4.22.2 Baseline policy under limited compute

Because time and compute are constrained, the baseline set should be deliberately narrow.

The **minimum defensible baseline package** is:

1. `Base`, because every claim must be anchored to the host model's native behavior.
2. `VCD`, because it is the most recognizable two-branch decode-time contrast baseline and directly clarifies IFCB's same-world argument.
3. `DAMO`, because it is the closest recent layer-dynamics baseline and therefore the strongest check on whether IFCB adds value beyond cross-layer stabilization alone.

`DeCo` should be treated as the **first optional upgrade**, not as a mandatory blocker. If it is included, it strengthens the paper. If it is omitted for budget reasons, the manuscript can still remain coherent as long as `Base + VCD + DAMO + IFCB` are fully executed and the omission is not disguised.

Methods that require training, external retrieval, additional verifier pipelines, or substantially different engineering assumptions should be cited and discussed, but do not need to be run as primary baselines in a compute-constrained first submission.

### 4.22.3 Required ablations

The ablation package should not stop at formula fragments. It should test both **mechanism necessity** and **engineering sensitivity**.

The required ablations are:

1. **Factor ablations**: `U` only, `\Pi` only, `G` only, all pairwise combinations, and full IFCB.
2. **Probe-band sensitivity**: a narrow, medium, and slightly wider probe-layer band, to show that the effect is not an artifact of a single hand-picked layer window.
3. **Frontier sensitivity**: at least two `top-K` settings, to show that IFCB is not overfit to one frontier width.
4. **Penalty-strength sensitivity**: moderate variation in the decoding penalty coefficient, to show that gains are not coming from a brittle over-penalization regime.

Among these, the factor ablations are non-negotiable. The sensitivity experiments are strongly recommended because they help reviewers trust that IFCB is a stable decoding rule rather than a narrowly tuned heuristic.

### 4.22.4 Efficiency and runtime reporting

The paper should not claim an unrealistically tiny cost unless that number is directly measured. Instead, it should report concrete runtime evidence.

The mandatory efficiency metrics are:

- average per-sample or per-token decoding latency,
- peak memory usage,
- relative slowdown versus `Base`,
- and, if convenient, throughput under the same decoding setting.

These measurements should be reported at least for:

- `Base`,
- `VCD`,
- `DAMO`,
- `IFCB`.

The purpose of this table is not to prove that IFCB is the cheapest method in existence. The purpose is to show that IFCB remains a practical zero-training decode-time intervention and that its added cost is justified by the empirical gain. In particular, the paper should avoid unverified statements such as "overhead <5%" unless the exact measurement supports them.

### 4.22.5 Expected empirical reading

The target empirical conclusion should be framed realistically.

For a strong ACM MM submission, the most important outcome pattern is:

- IFCB clearly outperforms `Base`,
- IFCB clearly outperforms `VCD` on the main hallucination benchmarks,
- IFCB is at least competitive with, and ideally somewhat better than, `DAMO`,
- IFCB does not collapse `MME` and `MMBench` capability scores.

If `DeCo` is also run, the practical goal is not necessarily to dominate it everywhere, but to remain competitive while preserving a cleaner same-forward, commitment-theoretic interpretation.

This means the paper should not overpromise universal superiority. The more defensible claim is that IFCB offers a **cleaner and more complete commitment-control rule** whose empirical behavior is strongest precisely where hallucination appears as unsupported semantic commitment.

### 4.22.6 Recommended execution order

To reduce wasted compute, the experiments should be run in the following **priority order**, with `LLaVA-1.5` used first as the main development host and `InstructBLIP` used second as the transfer-validation host.

**Priority 1: Make sure IFCB works at all on the main host**

1. fixed-slice smoke tests on `LLaVA-1.5` for `POPE`, `CHAIR`, and `HallusionBench`,
2. `POPE` three-split comparison on `LLaVA-1.5` with `Base`, `VCD`, `DAMO`, and `IFCB`.

This is the first real gate. If IFCB does not show a convincing gain over `Base` and preferably over `VCD` here, the rest of the pipeline should be reconsidered before more compute is spent.

**Priority 2: Establish the full main story on the main host**

3. `CHAIR` on `LLaVA-1.5`,
4. `HallusionBench` on `LLaVA-1.5`.

After these four steps, the paper should already have its main single-host hallucination narrative.

**Priority 3: Verify that the mechanism transfers across hosts**

5. fixed-slice smoke tests on `InstructBLIP`,
6. `POPE` three-split comparison on `InstructBLIP` with `Base`, `VCD`, `DAMO`, and `IFCB`,
7. `CHAIR` on `InstructBLIP`,
8. `HallusionBench` on `InstructBLIP`.

This block is critical because it turns IFCB from a single-host result into a more credible decode-time mechanism claim across two distinct open-weight MLLM families.

**Priority 4: Prove that the IFCB factorization matters**

9. factor ablations on the stronger or more stable of the two hosts,
10. if affordable, mirror the most important ablation subset on the second host.

The first host chosen for ablation should be the one with cleaner and more stable benchmark gains, not automatically the one tested first.

**Priority 5: Check capability retention**

11. `MME` and `MMBench` on `LLaVA-1.5`,
12. essential capability-retention confirmation on `InstructBLIP`, at minimum `Base` vs `IFCB`.

These experiments are important, but they come after the main hallucination claim is established, because capability retention only matters once there is a real hallucination gain worth retaining.

**Priority 6: Strengthen the paper after the core claim is already secure**

13. runtime and efficiency table across `Base`, `VCD`, `DAMO`, and `IFCB`,
14. qualitative case studies with `U`, `\Pi`, `G`, and `R` visualization,
15. optional `DeCo`,
16. optional probe-band, frontier, and penalty-strength sensitivity expansions.

This order is intentionally asymmetric: first prove the main effect on `LLaVA-1.5`, then prove transfer to `InstructBLIP`, then prove mechanism, then prove cost/retention, and only after that spend extra compute on optional strengthening experiments.

### 4.22.7 What is mandatory for the first complete paper version

For the first submission-quality version of the manuscript, the truly necessary empirical package is:

- `Base + VCD + DAMO + IFCB`,
- the two fixed host models `LLaVA-1.5` and `InstructBLIP`,
- `POPE` all three splits on both hosts,
- `CHAIR` on both hosts,
- `HallusionBench` on both hosts,
- at least one capability-retention table on `MME` and `MMBench`,
- one full factor-ablation table,
- one runtime/efficiency table,
- one compact qualitative figure.

Everything beyond this package should be treated as strengthening evidence rather than as the minimum threshold for a credible paper.

### 4.22.8 Must-complete experiments before the ACM MM abstract deadline (`3.25`)

Because the abstract deadline arrives before the full paper is complete, the pre-deadline goal should be to secure the **minimum evidence needed to support the abstract narrative without overclaiming**.

The non-negotiable experiments before `3.25` are:

1. **Pipeline sanity on both hosts.**  
   Run fixed-slice smoke tests for `LLaVA-1.5` and `InstructBLIP` on `POPE`, `CHAIR`, and `HallusionBench` to ensure the IFCB runtime is stable and metrics are wired correctly.

2. **Main proof-of-effect on the primary host.**  
   Complete the full `POPE` three-split comparison on `LLaVA-1.5` with `Base`, `VCD`, `DAMO`, and `IFCB`. This is the single most important pre-deadline result and the first hard gate for whether the current algorithm is paper-viable.

3. **Open-generation validation on the primary host.**  
   Complete `CHAIR` on `LLaVA-1.5` with at least `Base` and `IFCB`, and preferably also `VCD` and `DAMO`. This is necessary to show that IFCB is not only helping yes/no object decisions but also suppressing unsupported object commitments in free-form generation.

4. **Cross-host transfer check.**  
   Complete at least the core `POPE` comparison on `InstructBLIP`, ideally with `Base`, `VCD`, `DAMO`, and `IFCB`. If budget becomes critical, `Base` vs `IFCB` is the absolute minimum fallback, but the preferred version keeps the same baseline set as `LLaVA-1.5`.

5. **One preliminary fine-grained reasoning check.**  
   Run at least a stable preliminary `HallusionBench` evaluation on `LLaVA-1.5`. A full clean run is preferred, but before the abstract deadline the most important requirement is to verify that IFCB does not immediately collapse under visually delicate reasoning settings.

6. **One minimal efficiency readout.**  
   Measure rough latency and memory overhead on `LLaVA-1.5` for `Base` and `IFCB`, and preferably for `VCD` and `DAMO` as well. Even a compact pre-deadline runtime table is valuable because it prevents the abstract and introduction from making unsupported efficiency claims.

7. **One qualitative case package.**  
   Prepare at least one clean `POPE` case and one clean `CHAIR` case showing the behavior of `U`, `\Pi`, `G`, and `R`. These examples will help immediately when writing the final abstract, introduction, and method figure captions.

The experiments that may be deferred until after `3.25`, if necessary, are:

- the full `HallusionBench` run on both hosts,
- full `MME` and `MMBench`,
- complete factor ablations,
- sensitivity studies,
- optional `DeCo`,
- and a polished full runtime table.

In short, before `3.25` the team must first prove **one strong host effect** (`LLaVA-1.5`), **one transfer signal** (`InstructBLIP`), **one generation result** (`CHAIR`), and **one fine-grained reasoning sanity check** (`HallusionBench`). Everything else strengthens the paper, but these are the minimum results that should exist before the abstract is locked.
