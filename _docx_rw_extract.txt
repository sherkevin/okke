Related Work: Calibrating Hallucinations via Object-Resonant Decoding in Multimodal Large Language Models
1. Introduction to the Multimodal Hallucination Bottleneck
The advent of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has fundamentally transformed the trajectory of artificial intelligence research, expanding the capabilities of machine learning systems from unimodal text processing to sophisticated, cross-modal visual reasoning. By grafting advanced vision encoders onto highly parameterized Large Language Model (LLM) backbones, these architectures have achieved unprecedented fluency in tasks such as complex image captioning, visual question answering (VQA), and nuanced multimodal dialogue. However, as the architectural complexity and parameter scale of these models have expanded, a critical and pervasive failure mode has emerged: multimodal hallucination.
In the specific context of MLLMs, hallucination is formally defined as the generation of linguistic content that is syntactically coherent, grammatically flawless, and contextually fluent, yet entirely factually inconsistent with, or unsupported by, the provided visual input.1 As these models transition from experimental curiosities to foundational components in high-stakes, real-world deployments—ranging from medical imaging diagnostics and autonomous driving to embodied AI and robotic manipulation—the mitigation of such unfaithful generations has escalated from a theoretical optimization problem to an urgent safety imperative.3 A fluent but factually incorrect assertion by a medical MLLM can lead to severe misdiagnoses, while a hallucinated object in an autonomous driving context can result in catastrophic physical failures.3
This comprehensive report synthesizes the expansive body of literature generated between 2024 and 2026 addressing the evaluation, causation, and mitigation of MLLM hallucinations. By systematically deconstructing the underlying mechanics of modality bias, the evolution of decode-time interventions, and the rise of tool-augmented verification frameworks, this analysis establishes the critical gaps in current methodologies. Specifically, it exposes the widespread failure to address the fundamental "cross-modal granularity mismatch" at the sub-word tokenizer level, the latency bottlenecks introduced by multi-pass decoding strategies, and the fragility of heuristic-based post-hoc corrections. In doing so, it provides the theoretical and empirical foundation for CHORD (Calibrating Hallucinations via Object-Resonant Decoding), a training-free, decode-time intervention that leverages a novel Tokenizer Bridge and a purely mathematical Resonance Delta () to dynamically and efficiently neutralize hallucinations.
2. Hallucinations in Multimodal Large Language Models
The phenomenon of object and relational hallucinations in MLLMs is not a random stochastic error, but rather a systematic architectural byproduct of how modern multimodal systems are constructed and trained. To understand the prevalence and persistence of these hallucinations, the literature has increasingly focused on the inherent imbalance between the visual and textual components of the model.
2.1 The Phenomenology and the Modality Gap
The foundational root of object hallucinations in MLLMs can be traced to a structural phenomenon widely identified in recent literature as the "modality gap" or "modality mismatch".5 Current state-of-the-art MLLM architectures—including prominent families such as LLaVA, Qwen-VL, InstructBLIP, and InternVL—are typically constructed by projecting the continuous representations of a frozen or partially fine-tuned vision encoder (such as CLIP or SigLIP) into the discrete embedding space of a massive, pre-trained LLM backbone. This scale and architectural mismatch creates a profound inherent imbalance: the generative reasoning engine is overwhelmingly dominated by the linguistic priors embedded within the LLM's vast parametric memory, effectively marginalizing the visual signal.4
During the autoregressive generation process, the decoding mechanism tends to over-rely on textual prompts and its internal statistical distribution of language, treating the visual tokens as secondary, transient context. For example, because the underlying LLM backbone has been exposed to billions of text documents where the word "banana" is overwhelmingly associated with the color "yellow," the model is statistically predisposed to hallucinate a "yellow banana" even when the accompanying visual input unambiguously depicts a "green banana".4 This exposure bias is deeply ingrained in the pre-training datasets, which often feature highly imbalanced object distributions, strict co-occurrence patterns, and stereotypical visual descriptions. Consequently, the model generates fluent but factually irrelevant descriptions, confidently asserting the presence of objects based on semantic context rather than empirical visual evidence.4
2.2 Causal and Statistical Mechanisms of Exposure Bias
Recent theoretical frameworks developed throughout 2024 and 2025 have attempted to rigorously map the causal mechanisms driving this bias. The CausalMM framework, introduced by Tong et al. (2024), applies structural causal modeling directly to MLLMs, explicitly treating modality priors as a mathematical confounder between the attention mechanism and the final generated output.10 By employing back-door adjustment and counterfactual reasoning at both the visual and language attention levels, CausalMM isolates the negative effects of linguistic priors.10 This causal analysis demonstrates that hallucination is not merely an error in visual perception or feature extraction, but a systemic causal failure in cross-modal alignment within the deeper transformer layers.
Furthermore, empirical observations by Zheng and Zhang (2025) reveal a highly complex phenomenon termed "modality bias." Their investigation utilizes attention lenses to demonstrate that MLLMs not only ignore visual information during a hallucinatory episode but simultaneously fail to properly attend to the textual modality as well.5 This indicates that MLLMs struggle to simultaneously attend to and balance both visual and textual modalities, leading to a fragmented, chaotic understanding of user-provided instructions where the model effectively "drifts" into its parametric memory.5 Additional research into "attention sinks" further supports this, showing that certain tokens absorb disproportionate attention weights, causing the model to lose focus on the grounded visual evidence and revert to high-probability linguistic continuations.4
2.3 The Evolution of Evaluation Benchmarks
As the understanding of the deep-seated mechanisms of hallucination has matured, the academic community has recognized that generic, heuristic mitigation strategies are insufficient. This recognition has driven a massive surge in the development of sophisticated, highly granular evaluation benchmarks between 2024 and 2026, transitioning from basic object counting to complex, adversarial probing.3
Early benchmarks, such as the Caption Hallucination Assessment with Image Relevance (CHAIR) metric, pioneered the evaluation of object hallucination by algorithmically measuring the proportion of generated words that map to ground-truth object segmentations or bounding boxes.12 However, the CHAIR metric, despite its historical importance, suffers from profound instability issues. It relies on strict lexical matching and fails to capture the nuanced dynamics, synonymy, and multi-turn contextual reasoning of modern conversational MLLMs.12 To address these limitations, the Polling-based Object Probing Evaluation (POPE) simplified the detection process by reframing hallucination assessment as a series of discriminative, balanced yes-or-no questions regarding the presence of objects.12
While POPE established a reliable baseline for object-level hallucination, it predominantly evaluates single-object presence in relatively simple scenes, struggling to assess complex relational fabrications, attribute-level errors, or adversarial contexts.12 Consequently, the latest generation of benchmarks has introduced unprecedented rigor and scale.
The PhD (ChatGPT-Prompted Visual Hallucination Evaluation Dataset) benchmark, introduced by Liu et al. at CVPR 2025, represents a massive leap in objective, large-scale visual hallucination evaluation.15 Comprising over 102,000 Visual Question Answering (VQA) triplets, the PhD dataset evaluates MLLMs across five visual recognition tasks—ranging from low-level object and attribute recognition to middle-level sentiment, position recognition, and counting.16 Crucially, PhD introduces distinct, adversarial evaluation modes, testing models not only against normal visual questions but also against specious contexts (PhD-sec), incorrect contexts (PhD-icc), and AI-generated counter-common-sense images (PhD-ccs) designed to explicitly trigger linguistic priors.16
Similarly, the FINER benchmark (Xiao et al., 2026) isolates the specific and highly dangerous failure mode of false-positive hallucinations—where MLLMs stubbornly fail to answer "No" to negative queries. FINER systematically probes models with fine-grained negative queries that ask about non-existent image content across seven different granularity levels, exposing severe vulnerabilities even in advanced, highly scaled models like InternVL3.5-14B.12 Further pushing the boundaries of spatial localization, the HalLoc dataset (Park et al., CVPR 2025) provides token-level localization of hallucinations, allowing researchers to pinpoint the exact sub-word token where the generative trajectory diverges from visual reality.20

2.4 The Pivot: The Intractable Cross-Modal Granularity Mismatch
While the aforementioned literature provides critical empirical insights into the statistical causes of hallucinations (e.g., exposure bias, attention sinks) and offers highly robust frameworks for their evaluation, a fundamental architectural flaw remains virtually unaddressed in current mitigation strategies. Most contemporary analyses focus almost exclusively on macro-level data distribution, soft attention map alignment, or semantic language priors.4 However, this perspective entirely ignores the underlying, mechanical "cross-modal granularity mismatch" that occurs at the lowest, most foundational level of the model's architecture: the sub-word tokenizer.24
Vision encoders, whether based on traditional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) or modern Vision Transformers (ViTs), inherently process and output continuous, global feature representations. Even when an image is discretized into discrete visual patches, these patches represent holistic, unbroken spatial realities. Conversely, the LLM backbone is fundamentally bound by its text tokenizer (e.g., Byte-Pair Encoding or WordPiece), which splinters holistic semantic concepts into discrete, fragmented, and often counter-intuitive sub-word tokens. For instance, a cohesive visual concept like a "playground" might be aggressively tokenized into the fragmented sub-words ['play', '##ground'].25
When an MLLM attempts to ground a continuous visual entity (a whole object in the image) into a sequence of fragmented sub-word tokens, it creates a perilous "open-loop" decoding state.24 The visual signal may strongly support the generation of the first sub-word token of an object. However, once that first token is committed to the sequence, the immense autoregressive weight of the LLM's language model instantly takes over. The model's internal probability distribution shifts violently toward completing the linguistic sequence based purely on text priors (e.g., completing play with ##ground regardless of whether the rest of the object is actually visible), effectively abandoning the visual context.24
This granularity mismatch—continuous whole images forced through the bottleneck of discrete, fragmented sub-word tokens—is the root physical mechanism of linguistic inertia. Because the visual context cannot dynamically intervene mid-word or across fragmented, meaningless token spans, the model systematically "drifts" into a hallucination.26 Existing surveys and mitigation strategies from 2024 to 2026 broadly recognize the symptom—language prior over-reliance—but completely misdiagnose the physical cause, treating the generated text as atomic, whole words rather than tokenizer-bound fragments.1 It is precisely this unaddressed cross-modal granularity mismatch that our proposed framework, CHORD, mathematically resolves through a training-free Tokenizer Bridge intervention.
3. Decode-Time Intervention and Calibration
As the architectural limitations of MLLMs have become apparent, the realization that hallucinations are deeply and mechanically tied to the autoregressive decoding mechanism has sparked a massive shift in mitigation strategies. Historically, aligning these massive models relied heavily on computationally prohibitive techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) or massive, data-intensive visual instruction tuning.8 However, as highlighted in the most recent 2024–2026 literature, training-free, decode-time interventions have rapidly emerged as the most promising, scalable, and resource-efficient alternative.8 These strategies bypass the need for expensive parameter updates, attempting instead to mathematically calibrate the output probability distributions on the fly, actively penalizing linguistic bias and forcing the model to heavily weight its visual inputs during token generation.
3.1 Contrastive Decoding: Logit-Level and Attention-Steerable Paradigms
Contrastive Decoding (CD) has rapidly become a cornerstone technique for hallucination mitigation in the decode-time space.14 The fundamental premise of the CD paradigm involves contrasting the output logits of a standard, visually grounded forward pass against a "negative" or "hallucinated" forward pass. By subtracting the probability distribution of the negatively biased pass from the standard pass, the model theoretically penalizes its own linguistic priors and amplifies the tokens driven purely by the visual signal.
Methods such as Visual Contrastive Decoding (VCD) (Leng et al., CVPR 2024) and Image Contrastive Decoding (ICD) generate these negative samples by artificially injecting statistical noise into the visual input, or by providing heavily distorted or blank visual contexts to force the model to rely solely on language.28 While empirically effective at reducing certain distinct classes of visual hallucinations, logit-level contrastive decoding introduces severe instability into the text generation process. By adjusting the decoding distribution blindly at the final logit level, VCD frequently compromises output coherence, grammatical fluency, and lexical diversity. In some instances, it can even exacerbate hallucinations by introducing uncontrollable noise into the CD calculation, resulting in highly unnatural, disjointed phrasing.29
Recognizing the severe coherence degradation inherent in logit-level subtraction, subsequent 2025 literature shifted the contrastive operation away from the output logits and directly into the internal attention mechanisms. Attention Contrastive Decoding (ACD) and Attention-Steerable Contrastive Decoding (ASCD) (Wang et al., 2025) explicitly modulate internal attention distributions rather than final probabilities.30 ACD, for instance, employs an Adaptive Subtraction Strategy (ASS) designed to selectively identify and suppress hallucination-prone attention patterns directly within the transformer layers. This approach effectively leverages the inherent continuity of the attention mechanisms to preserve the model's linguistic diversity and fluency while significantly reducing factual inconsistency, avoiding the strict penalty mechanisms required by logit-level CD.30 Similarly, Retrieval Contrastive Decoding (RCD) investigates the respective statistical impacts of the visual and textual input branches, utilizing external knowledge retrieval to mathematically ground the contrastive step without destroying textual coherence.33
3.2 Inter-Layer and Attention-Guided Architectural Interventions
Parallel to the evolution of contrastive decoding, a highly active and theoretically dense area of 2025–2026 research focuses on internal layer representation and attention-guided intervention. These methodologies exploit a fascinating empirical phenomenon: MLLMs often possess the correct, factually grounded visual information in their intermediate transformer layers, but this vital knowledge is systematically overwritten, suppressed, or ignored by overpowering language priors as the signal propagates to the final output layers.34
A seminal early approach for standard text-only LLMs, Decoding by Contrasting Layers (DoLa), applies layer-wise contrastive decoding to actively penalize premature linguistic convergence.37 By contrasting the final layer with early or middle layers, DoLa forces the model to emphasize late-stage reasoning. However, DoLa targets pure text architectures and performs notoriously poorly on multimodal tasks. It actively degrades performance in vision-language benchmarks because the heuristic cannot differentiate between textual logical progression and cross-modal visual grounding.29 To successfully adapt this inter-layer concept for MLLMs, Wang et al. (ICLR 2025) proposed DeCo (Dynamic Correction Decoding). DeCo adaptively selects the appropriate preceding transformer layers and proportionally integrates their knowledge into the final layer, dynamically adjusting the output logits based specifically on where the visual object was most strongly recognized within the network's depth.34
Expanding on this internal monitoring, the NAACL 2025 paper by Xu et al. introduced Image Token Attention-Guided Decoding (iTaD), providing a highly nuanced plug-and-play decoding method. iTaD makes the critical core observation that hallucinatory outputs in MLLMs strictly correlate with a measurable reduction in attention weights directed from the generated output tokens toward the input image tokens.38 To capitalize on this, iTaD constructs an "Image Token Attention Vector" (iTaV) for each decoding layer. It employs a dynamic selection strategy, continuously calculating the distance between the iTaV of an intermediate layer and the final layer. It then performs inter-layer contrastive decoding by subtracting the probability distribution of the most distant intermediate layer.38 Unlike DoLa, which relies on static, pre-defined layers, iTaD’s dynamic, interaction-based selection highlights the actual computational progression of image understanding.38
Another distinct architectural intervention is OPERA (Huang et al., 2024), which acts directly on the attention mechanism by implementing a severe overconfidence penalty and a hindsight allocation strategy. OPERA algorithmically detects instances where a model's internal attention weights suggest an unwarranted level of certainty that is entirely unbacked by corresponding visual evidence. Upon detection, it forces the beam search algorithm to halt, rollback, and reconsider previous tokens, breaking the chain of linguistic inertia.13
Conversely, MemVR (Memory-Space Visual Retracing), introduced by Zou et al. at ICML 2025, tackles the specific "amnesia" that MLLM text decoders experience regarding early visual information.29 Inspired by human cognitive processes—specifically the behavior where an individual "looks twice" at a scene when a memory fades—MemVR mathematically re-injects visual tokens directly into the MLLM's Feed Forward Network (FFN) as a "key-value memory" at a designated middle trigger layer.29 This visual retracing is dynamically triggered exclusively when the model exhibits high mathematical uncertainty during inference.29
Furthermore, the "Seeing Right but Saying Wrong" phenomenon, extensively explored in the DualPD framework (Song et al., arXiv 2026), provides a dual-perspective decoding refinement strategy.36 DualPD tracks the "visual attention trajectory" across both entire transformer layers and individual attention heads. It applies intra-layer filtering to suppress the influence of noisy, uninformative heads while contrasting logits between the specific layers exhibiting the largest shift in attention. This highly complex internal manipulation successfully guides the MLLM to focus precisely on task-relevant image regions without requiring any parameter fine-tuning.36
3.3 Sub-Word Ensembling and Stability Interventions
Recognizing the instability of generating fragmented sub-word tokens, frameworks like SAFE (Stable And Fast LLM Ensembling), proposed by KAIST in late 2025, attempt to stabilize the generation process at the token level.27 SAFE consolidates probabilities spread across multiple sub-word tokens that represent the same whole word into a single representative token to avoid Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) mismatches and prevent early hallucinatory divergence.27 However, this framework relies on a computationally heavy "generate-verify-ensemble" pipeline requiring drafting and verification models, severely limiting its speed and practical deployment in resource-constrained environments.45
3.4 The Pivot: Breaking Universality Boundaries and Latency Bottlenecks
Despite the profound theoretical elegance and empirical success of methods like OPERA, iTaD, DeCo, DualPD, and SAFE, an aggressive critique reveals that they suffer from three catastrophic architectural flaws that severely limit their real-world deployment and scalability.
First, these methods are intrinsically and heavily coupled to model-internal states. DeCo requires precise extraction and manipulation of hidden states across specific, hard-coded layer indices.35 iTaD depends on extracting and continually computing the iTaV from dense, massive attention matrices.38 OPERA actively manipulates internal beam search routing mechanisms based on attention overconfidence.38 This deep architectural coupling fundamentally shatters the "universality boundary" across heterogeneous models. Applying these intrusive techniques to novel, proprietary, or uniquely structured black-box MLLMs (where internal attention maps and hidden transformer layers are inaccessible or structurally different) is mathematically and practically impossible.
Second, contrastive and inter-layer methods inherently suffer from severe latency bottlenecks. Standard contrastive decoding (like VCD and ICD) physically requires running two distinct forward passes per token generation—one standard pass and one computationally identical corrupted pass.29 Even highly optimized internal methods struggle with massive computational overhead. For instance, the authors of iTaD (NAACL 2025) note that while their method only increases inference latency by a factor of 1.08 to 1.18, OPERA results in a multiple-fold increase in inference latency due to its highly complex hindsight allocation and beam-search rollback mechanisms.38 In an era where real-time, high-throughput inference is an absolute prerequisite for autonomous systems, robotics, and edge devices, doubling or quadrupling the computational cost via multi-pass autoregressive generation is entirely intractable.
Third, and perhaps most dangerously, these internal decoding strategies heavily rely on naive entropy or uncertainty gating. For example, MemVR only triggers its protective "look twice" mechanism when the model explicitly exhibits "high uncertainty" via a flattened probability distribution.29 However, empirical evidence demonstrates that the most dangerous and misleading hallucinations are not those generated with uncertainty; they are "overconfidence hallucinations." In these instances, the model’s biased language priors drive it to fabricate an object with absolute mathematical certainty (often exhibiting near 1.0 probability on the hallucinated token).46 Naive entropy-based triggers systematically fail to catch these instances because the LLM is entirely confident in its erroneous generation, completely bypassing the intervention gates.46
Our proposed method, CHORD, violently breaks away from these constrained paradigms. By operating purely at the output probability space using Continuous Top-1 Monitoring integrated with the KV-Cache, CHORD achieves near-zero inference latency. It requires absolutely no internal access to hidden layers, attention matrices, or beam search manipulations, perfectly preserving universal applicability across any autoregressive MLLM. Most importantly, by calculating a pure mathematical Resonance Delta (), CHORD actively neutralizes overconfidence hallucinations by intervening regardless of the LLM's internal entropy, dynamically penalizing unwarranted certainty without the massive computational overhead of full-K evaluations or multi-pass forward operations.
4. Visual Grounding and Tool-Augmented Verification
To bypass the severe internal computational complexities of attention manipulation and contrastive decoding, a highly prolific and distinct branch of research advocates for tool-augmented verification. This paradigm structurally integrates MLLMs with highly specialized, external foundational models—such as object detectors, segmentation networks, or vision-language retrieval models (e.g., Grounding DINO, CLIP, SAM)—to verify, constrain, or post-edit the generated outputs.48 These hybrid systems attempt to leverage the vast open-world knowledge and fluid reasoning capabilities of LLMs while anchoring their specific factual claims to the strict, deterministic, and highly localized outputs of narrow vision experts.50
4.1 Post-Hoc Correction and the Fragility of NLP Heuristics
The most intuitive and widely deployed application of tool-augmentation is post-hoc hallucination correction. Frameworks operating in this space allow the MLLM to generate its full response freely and uninterrupted, subsequently running a complex diagnostic pipeline on the final text to identify and correct factual inconsistencies before presenting the output to the user.51 A premier, state-of-the-art example of this paradigm is Woodpecker (Yin et al., Sci China Inf Sci 2024), a comprehensive training-free framework that executes a rigorous five-stage diagnostic pipeline.28
The Woodpecker methodology first employs an LLM to algorithmically extract key concepts (specifically physical objects) from the generated text. It then formulates targeted, highly specific questions about these extracted concepts, and validates their existence, count, and physical attributes using "expert models." Specifically, it relies on Grounding DINO for highly accurate open-set object detection and bounding box generation, and BLIP-2 for Visual Question Answering (VQA) to verify attributes like color and relative position.51 Based on the output of these external tools, Woodpecker generates a structured visual knowledge base of verified claims and forces an LLM to rewrite the hallucinated portions of the original response, explicitly injecting bounding box coordinates as hard grounding evidence.51
While Woodpecker demonstrates significant empirical gains—improving MiniGPT-4's accuracy from 54.67% to an impressive 85.33% on the POPE benchmark 51—the post-hoc architectural approach is inherently flawed. Post-remedy frameworks are fundamentally bound by the performance ceilings and failure modes of their chosen expert models. If the VQA model provides incorrect spatial reasoning, or the object detector misses an occlusion, the correction fails entirely, or worse, introduces new hallucinations.51
Furthermore, post-hoc methods are severely plagued by high omission and mis-correction rates due to error propagation. Extensive breakdown tests reveal that the Woodpecker framework entirely fails to correct 14.6% of erroneous responses and mistakenly corrupts 6.2% of originally correct, factually accurate responses. This degradation occurs because errors in early pipeline stages (such as failing to extract a key concept) geometrically propagate through the subsequent validation and correction stages.51
Crucially, systems like Woodpecker rely heavily on brittle, heuristic Natural Language Processing (NLP) rules. They utilize external code libraries like spacy and POS (Part-Of-Speech) taggers (en_core_web_sm) to attempt to extract nouns and entities from the raw generated text.51 If these heuristic text analyzers misidentify a complex syntactic structure, fail to extract a nuanced noun phrase, or encounter domain-specific jargon, the entire visual validation pipeline breaks down before it even issues a query to the image.51
4.2 Early-Intervention and Preference-Tuned Tooling
To completely avoid the compounding errors and fragility of post-hoc correction, researchers in 2025 and 2026 have aggressively moved toward early-intervention and training-based tool augmentation paradigms.
The SENTINEL (Sentence-level Early Intervention through In-domain Preference Learning) framework, introduced by Peng et al. at ICCV 2025, represents a massive evolutionary step in this space.53 SENTINEL operates on the critical empirical insight that hallucinations predominantly emerge at the very beginning of text generation (often within the first few words of a sentence) and subsequently geometrically propagate through all subsequent outputs due to the autoregressive nature of the model.54
To counter this early onset, SENTINEL abandons post-hoc editing entirely in favor of training the model to recognize and avoid hallucinations at the sentence level during generation.54 The framework automatically bootstraps high-quality, in-domain preference pairs by repeatedly sampling model outputs and validating object existence through rigorous cross-checking with two separate open-vocabulary detectors.54 It categorizes the generated sentences into strict hallucinated and non-hallucinated buckets, constructing massive datasets of context-aware preference data. Finally, the underlying MLLM is fine-tuned using a novel Context-aware Direct Preference Optimization (C-DPO) loss function, which aggressively emphasizes discriminative learning exactly at the token boundaries where hallucinations initially manifest.54 SENTINEL achieved state-of-the-art performance, reducing hallucinations by over 90% compared to baseline models without severely compromising generative diversity.54
Recent 2026 works, such as the SToRM (Supervised Token Reduction for Multi-modal LLMs) framework by Kim et al., further refine deep tool integration for highly specialized, latency-sensitive domains like end-to-end autonomous driving.56 The SToRM architecture utilizes a lightweight importance predictor (designed via an MLP-Mixer block with a temporal sliding window) to dynamically capture complex spatio-temporal relations among visual tokens.58 It proposes an Anchor-Context Merging (ACM) module that dynamically reduces visual tokens based on pseudo-supervision signals derived from a full-token pass through the LLM.58 By intelligently reducing token redundancy guided by an external mechanism, SToRM achieves high-efficiency multimodal processing while strictly maintaining task fidelity and lowering computational overhead by up to 30x.57 Similarly, frameworks like MAPLE (Modality-Aware Post-training and Learning Ecosystem, 2026) tackle modality mismatch during reinforcement learning post-training by adaptively weighting signals from different tools to narrow the severe uni/multi-modal accuracy gap.60

4.3 The Pivot: Real-Time Resonance Delta vs. Spatial Misalignment
While existing tool-augmented methods showcase the immense, undeniable value of utilizing external foundational models, seamlessly incorporating them into an MLLM without creating a disjointed, highly fragile "Frankenstein" system is profoundly challenging. As demonstrated in the literature, current external tool methods suffer from three fatal flaws: they are either (1) strictly post-hoc, which is fundamentally too late to alter the autoregressive generation trajectory without causing massive computational waste 51; (2) heavily reliant on brittle, rule-based NLP heuristics and POS taggers to arbitrarily decide what constitutes an "object" within a generated string 51; or (3) bound by expensive, compute-heavy preference-tuning constraints (like SENTINEL) that completely negate the widespread appeal of plug-and-play, training-free architectures.54
Furthermore, when tool-augmented models attempt real-time or near-real-time verification, they consistently suffer from severe "spatial misalignment." This phenomenon occurs because standard architectures attempt to match entire, partially generated text prefixes (which contain complex syntax, adjectives, and verbs) to highly local, specific bounding boxes. As the text prefix grows during generation, the overall semantic representation becomes heavily convoluted, and the external vision tool (e.g., CLIP or DINO) completely fails to align the complex, noisy textual context with the specific physical boundaries of the image.
CHORD decisively and elegantly bypasses these architectural dead ends. Instead of waiting for an NLP heuristic to parse a completed sentence, or relying on multi-pass DPO preference training, CHORD intervenes directly and immediately at the discrete decoding step. It dynamically and precisely amplifies the output logits of sub-word tokens that physically resonate with grounded entities in the image, while mathematically penalizing tokens that diverge into hallucinations. Because this highly targeted intervention occurs purely through the Resonance Delta at the sub-word bridge, CHORD requires absolutely zero POS tagging, zero post-hoc editing, and zero fine-tuning. 5. Synthesis and Conclusion
The extensive survey of the 2024–2026 multimodal landscape reveals a clear, highly active progression in the fight against MLLM hallucinations. The academic community has rigorously established the undeniable causal links between exposure bias, modality gaps, and the overwhelming force of linguistic inertia.6 This deep understanding has given rise to highly sophisticated, adversarial evaluation benchmarks like PhD, FINER, and HalLoc, which stress-test models across fine-grained negative queries, counter-common-sense contexts, and token-level localization.12
To systematically address these generative failures without incurring the prohibitive, massive computational costs of full-model retraining, decode-time interventions—spanning contrastive paradigms (VCD, ACD, ASCD) and complex internal attention manipulations (OPERA, MemVR, iTaD, DualPD)—have entirely dominated the recent literature.29 Simultaneously, tool-augmented verification frameworks like Woodpecker and SENTINEL have empirically proven the indispensable, concrete value of integrating external foundational models like Grounding DINO to definitively anchor factual claims.51
Yet, despite this massive volume of research, a critical and dangerous void remains at the exact intersection of these advanced paradigms. Existing decoding strategies fatally break the universality boundary by requiring invasive access to proprietary hidden states. They cripple inference speeds with multi-pass autoregressive generation, and they systematically fail to neutralize the dangerous "overconfidence hallucinations" that easily evade naive entropy gating.38 Conversely, external tool integrations are relegated to brittle, heuristic-laden post-hoc editing or expensive preference-tuning pipelines that cannot be deployed dynamically across diverse models.51
Most critically, the entire body of cited literature has treated generated text as atomic words, fundamentally ignoring the "cross-modal granularity mismatch" between continuous visual encodings and discrete, fragmented sub-word tokens.25 It is within this exact architectural void that CHORD operates. By introducing a mathematically sound Tokenizer Bridge and a purely mathematical Resonance Delta (), CHORD provides a 100% training-free, universally plug-and-play intervention. It neutralizes overconfidence hallucinations with near-zero latency via Continuous Top-1 Monitoring, seamlessly and elegantly marrying the spatial authority of external foundational models with the real-time, sub-word generative physics of large language models.
